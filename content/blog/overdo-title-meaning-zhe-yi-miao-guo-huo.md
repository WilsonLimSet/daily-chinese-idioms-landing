The English title *Overdo* is a neat one-word translation, but it only carries half of what the Chinese title says. The drama's real name is **这一秒过火 (Zhè yī miǎo guò huǒ)**, and the key word — **过火** — is doing clever double duty. Here's the breakdown.

## Character by character

- **这 (zhè)** — this
- **一秒 (yī miǎo)** — one second; a moment. (一 = one, 秒 = second.)
- **过火 (guò huǒ)** — literally "**over / past the fire**": 过 means to pass, cross, or exceed, and 火 means fire.

Read literally, **这一秒过火** is something like "**this moment catches fire**" or "in this second, it burns."

## The idiom hiding inside: 过火

Here's the clever part. **过火 (guò huǒ)** isn't just "over the fire" — it's a common expression meaning **"to overdo it, to go too far, to be excessive."** You'll hear it in everyday Mandarin:

> 你这个玩笑开得有点**过火**了。
> *Nǐ zhège wánxiào kāi de yǒudiǎn guòhuǒ le.*
> "That joke of yours went a bit **too far**."

So **过火** simultaneously means:
1. **Literal:** to burn, to catch fire — evoking passion, heat, intensity.
2. **Idiomatic:** to go overboard, to cross a line, to overdo it.

That's exactly why the English title is **Overdo** — it picks the idiomatic meaning. But the Chinese keeps *both*: this is a love-hate melodrama where feelings run so hot they **overheat** — a love that, in a single moment, **goes too far**. The fire is both the passion and the warning.

## Why it fits the drama

*Overdo* is built on an intense, painful love-hate dynamic — two people whose connection repeatedly burns them and everyone around them. A relationship that "过火" is one that has crossed from warmth into something scorching and dangerous. The title, in three quick syllables, tells you the emotional temperature before the first scene even starts.

## The takeaway

Chinese drama titles reward a second look: **过火** is a perfect example of how a single word can hold a literal image (fire) and a figurative judgment (excess) at once — nuance the English "Overdo" can only hint at.
