*Overdo* (这一秒过火) unfolds in one of the most glamorous and dangerous cities of the 20th century: **1920s Shanghai**. It's a setting Chinese dramas return to again and again — and for good reason. Here's the real history behind the qipao, the dance halls, and the intrigue.

## The "Paris of the East"

In the 1920s and '30s, Shanghai was the most cosmopolitan city in Asia — nicknamed the **"Paris of the East"** and the "Pearl of the Orient." It was a boomtown of banks, department stores, cinemas, cabarets, and neon, where Chinese modernity collided with the wider world. This is the world of the **qipao** (旗袍), the calendar-poster beauties, and the "modern girl."

## A city split into concessions

The reason 1920s Shanghai feels so unusual is that it wasn't governed as one city. After the Opium Wars, foreign powers carved out **concessions** — self-governing enclaves inside Shanghai:

- The **International Settlement** (British and American), centered on the famous waterfront, **the Bund (外滩)**, with its grand Western banks and hotels.
- The **French Concession**, known for its tree-lined avenues and villas.
- The Chinese-administered districts around them.

Crucially, foreigners in the concessions enjoyed **extraterritoriality** — they were largely outside Chinese law. That legal patchwork made Shanghai a magnet for money, refugees, revolutionaries, and criminals alike.

## The underworld: the Green Gang

Beneath the glamour ran a powerful criminal underworld. The **Green Gang (青帮)** — led most famously by **Du Yuesheng (杜月笙)** — controlled opium, gambling, and vice, and was deeply entangled with business and politics. In dramas set in this era, the gangster boss, the nightclub, and the uneasy deals between the powerful are drawn straight from this history. It's why a Republican-Shanghai romance so often carries an undertow of danger.

## A turbulent backdrop

The 1920s were also politically volatile: the warlord era, the rise of the Nationalists and Communists, and the shocks of 1927 all played out with Shanghai at the center. Fortunes were made and lost overnight, families rose and fell, and identity itself could be fragile — the perfect stage for a story like *Overdo*, built on a child **switched at birth**, a faked death, and a love that burns too hot.

## Why dramas love this era

Republican-era (**民国**) Shanghai gives storytellers everything at once: dazzling fashion and modern romance on the surface, and beneath it, gangs, politics, and the collision of old and new China. It's a world where a warlord's son and a woman living under a false name can cross paths — and where everything is always one moment away from going **过火** (too far).
