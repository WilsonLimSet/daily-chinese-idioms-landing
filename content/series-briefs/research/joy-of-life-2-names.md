# Joy of Life (庆余年) Character Names — Research Brief

**Scope:** Character-by-character decoding of names in Mao Ni's (猫腻) novel *Qìng Yú Nián* (《庆余年》) and the Tencent drama. For each character: exact hanzi, pinyin with tones, per-character meaning, combined connotation, literary reference, authorial intent vs. fan theory, and the arc connection.

**Why this matters:** Mao Ni is known for a split style — some names are loaded with classical allusion (叶轻眉, 司理理), others are openly homophone jokes (范建, 范思辙, 王启年). Both halves are part of the joke, and Chinese readers see both at once. This brief flags which is which.

---

## How Chinese Names Carry Meaning (Brief Primer)

A Chinese name is normally one surname (姓 xìng) plus one or two given-name characters (名 míng). Educated characters often also carry a 字 (zì, "courtesy name") chosen at coming-of-age, used by peers and superiors. Each hanzi is a morpheme: it has its own dictionary meaning, a poetic register, and a small cloud of classical associations (from poems, the *Book of Changes*, Tang dynasty figures, etc.). When a novelist names a character, readers in Chinese parse three layers at once:

1. **Surface meaning** of each hanzi (e.g., 闲 xián = "leisure").
2. **Allusion** to a poem, historical figure, or chengyu.
3. **Homophone (谐音 xiéyīn)** — what the name sounds like out loud, which can be a joke or a warning.

Mao Ni openly uses all three layers. He has admitted on social media that some names (especially the 范 family) are intentional homophone humor at his own expense, after readers mocked his naming. Other names (叶轻眉, 陈萍萍, 海棠朵朵) are deliberate poetic constructs — the title 《庆余年》 itself is from a *Dream of the Red Chamber* (《红楼梦》) chapter-five song, which in turn quotes the *I Ching*'s 坤·文言 ("积善之家，必有余庆 — A house that accumulates good will have surplus blessings"). The book's title is itself a pun on this idiom and the protagonist's "borrowed extra years" after rebirth.

## Character 1: Fan Xian (范闲)

- **Chinese:** 范闲
- **Pinyin:** Fàn Xián
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 范 (fàn): A common Chinese surname (32nd most common). Outside surname use, 范 means "model, example, pattern, mold" (as in 模范 mófàn "role model," 典范 diǎnfàn "paragon," 师范 shīfàn "teacher-training"). Originally the name of an ancient state and a craftsman's mold.
  - 闲 (xián): "Leisure, idle, free, unoccupied, at ease, tranquil, free from official duties." In classical literature 闲 is one of the highest aesthetic states — the recluse-poet's ideal (think 陶渊明's "闲适" leisure). Also "uninvolved, unconcerned." Modern compound: 清闲 qīngxián "free of cares."
- **Combined name meaning:** "An exemplary man at leisure" / "the model of unburdened ease." A Confucian-gentleman wish for a son who can stand apart from court politics.
- **Literary references:**
  - The phrase 闲云野鹤 ("idle cloud, wild crane") — the classical icon of a man who refuses official appointment. Fan Xian is constantly forced into the opposite role.
  - The homophone 犯嫌 (fàn xián) means "to offend, to be irritating, to draw enmity." Mao Ni explicitly leans into this — Fan Xian *is* an irritant to every faction.
- **Courtesy name (字):** 安之 Ān Zhī, granted by the Qing Emperor (his biological father). 安 = "peace, settle, content"; 之 = classical particle. Echoes the chengyu 安之若素 ānzhī-ruò-sù "to take things calmly, as if nothing had happened." Fan-noted interpretation: the Emperor's wish that Fan Xian "stay in his lane" and not reach for the throne. (Common interpretation, cited across Zhihu/Qidian.)
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Author-stated / cited in the novel:** The name is given by his mother Ye Qingmei before her death — she had foreseen danger and wanted him to live freely.
  - **Common interpretation:** 闲 expresses a mother's hope that her son live free, outside palace politics. The whole novel is the tragic irony that Fan Xian cannot remain 闲.
  - **Fan-theorized:** 犯嫌 homophone is intentional — Fan Xian is the irritant the Qing court cannot tolerate but cannot remove.
- **Connection to character arc:** A reborn modern man wants only to "live a leisurely extra life" (the literal meaning of 庆余年). His mother named him 闲 hoping he could; the entire plot is the universe refusing to let him.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 范闲 entry; Zhihu Q&A on 范闲 字 安之; Qidian answer page on the naming.

## Character 2: Lin Wan'er (林婉儿)

- **Chinese:** 林婉儿
- **Pinyin:** Lín Wǎn'ér
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 林 (lín): "Forest, grove." Common surname. Connotes lushness, scholarly families (翰林 hànlín = imperial academy).
  - 婉 (wǎn): "Gentle, graceful, tactful, demure, beautiful in a soft way." Classical female-virtue word — 婉约 wǎnyuē is the name of the gentler school of Song dynasty 词 (ci-poetry) associated with 李清照. 温婉 wēnwǎn = "warm and gentle."
  - 儿 (ér): Diminutive / affectionate suffix, especially common in northern Mandarin and Manchu-court usage for noblewomen's names. Roughly "-y" or "little —."
- **Combined name meaning:** "Gentle little [one] of the Lin forest" — a soft, classical, aristocratic-girl name.
- **Literary references:** 婉 is the keyword of the entire 婉约派 (graceful school) of Song poetry — Lin Wan'er's name is a one-character endorsement of the ideal demure-but-witty noblewoman of classical literature. Within the drama she is also called 鸡腿姑娘 ("Drumstick Girl") after the meet-cute scene.
- **Nickname (小名):** 依晨 Yī Chén ("relying on dawn / leaning on morning"), giving her the title 晨郡主 Chén Jùnzhǔ "Dawn Princess." (Note: Mao Ni has stated the character was modeled in part on the Taiwanese actress 林依晨 Ariel Lin — the in-universe 依晨 is a direct nod to the actress.)
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Author-stated:** Mao Ni said he based the character on actress Lin Yichen (林依晨), hence the small name 依晨.
  - **Common interpretation:** 婉儿 = the soft, classical foil to Fan Xian's modern wit. She is "out of the mud but unstained" (出淤泥而不染), a Zhou Dunyi (周敦颐) allusion fans frequently apply to her.
- **Connection to character arc:** Despite scandalous origins (illegitimate daughter of the Chancellor and the Eldest Princess), she remains gentle, honest, and uncorrupted by the court — literally living up to 婉.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 林婉儿; Qidian 林婉儿 character page; Zhihu thread on the character.

## Character 3: Yan Bingyun (言冰云)

- **Chinese:** 言冰云
- **Pinyin:** Yán Bīngyún
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 言 (yán): "Speech, word, to say." Also a relatively rare surname. In a spy-master's name, the irony is sharp: a man whose entire profession is *not* speaking.
  - 冰 (bīng): "Ice." Classical compound 冰心 bīngxīn ("ice-heart") = a pure, detached, incorruptible nature (from Wang Changling's 王昌龄 "一片冰心在玉壶" — "a heart of ice in a jade vase," the Tang-poetry icon of moral purity).
  - 云 (yún): "Cloud." In wuxia/xianxia naming convention 云 implies aloofness, distance, untouchability — the cloud that drifts above the affairs of men. Compound 云淡风轻 yúndàn-fēngqīng "clouds light, breeze faint" = utter detachment.
- **Combined name meaning:** "Speech [like] ice [and] cloud" — words that are cold, distant, and untouchable. Read as a whole: a man whose communication is glacial and remote.
- **Literary references:** 冰云 evokes the Tang/Song convention of comparing a noble character to ice + cloud (cold, pure, above worldly dust). The Wang Changling line above is the locus classicus.
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Author-cited:** Per multiple fan retellings of Mao Ni's web-novel-era comments, "Yan Bingyun" was originally just "Young Master Yan" (小言公子); a reader suggested the full name 冰云 and Mao Ni adopted it. He himself has joked that without the reader's help the name would have been 言冰冰 or 言云云 — fitting his usual reduplication-pun style.
  - **Common interpretation:** The 冰 and 云 perfectly index his on-page personality: surgically cold, emotionally unreachable, the perfect spymaster.
- **Connection to character arc:** Yan Bingyun is the genius spy who eventually inherits the Overwatch Council (监察院) from Chen Pingping. He is loyal to the Qing state, not to Fan Xian personally — a man as remote and uncompromising as ice and cloud.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 言冰云 entries (multiple); Qidian character page; Zhihu deep-dive 庆余年原著里的言冰云.

## Character 4: Qing Emperor (庆帝) — and the State of Qing (庆国)

- **Chinese:** 庆帝 / 庆国
- **Pinyin:** Qìng Dì / Qìng Guó
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 庆 (qìng): "Celebration, congratulation, auspicious blessing." Classical compound 余庆 yúqìng = "surplus blessing," the gift handed down to descendants of virtuous houses.
  - 帝 (dì): "Emperor."
  - 国 (guó): "State, country."
- **Combined name meaning:** "Emperor of the State of Celebration / Surplus Blessing." The state's name is the same 庆 used in the novel's title.
- **Literary references — this is the title-level pun:**
  - Source 1 (the *I Ching*): "积善之家，必有余庆；积不善之家，必有余殃 — A house that accumulates good will have surplus blessings; a house that accumulates evil will have surplus calamity." (《周易·坤·文言》)
  - Source 2 (*Dream of the Red Chamber*): The chapter-five song 《留余庆》 (Liú Yú Qìng "Leaving Behind Surplus Blessings") — Wang Xifeng once spared a poor relative; that relative later saves Wang's daughter Qiao'er. Mao Ni has cited this as a direct inspiration: Ye Qingmei did many kind acts; those people later protect her son Fan Xian.
  - Source 3 (Mao Ni himself, in interviews): the state name 庆 is also inspired by the State of Qing (庆国) in the Japanese fantasy series 《十二国记》 (*The Twelve Kingdoms*). (Cited across Baidu Baike, Sohu commentary.)
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Author-stated:** The book title is a direct lift from *Dream of the Red Chamber* + *I Ching*. The "surplus year(s)" the title celebrates are (a) Fan Xian's reborn life and (b) the inheritance of his mother's kindness.
  - **Fan-theorized:** A clever hanzi-decomposition theory — combine the top of 唐 (táng "Tang dynasty") with part of 太 (tài, as in 太宗 Tàizōng "Taizong") and you can argue you arrive at 庆. The novel's imperial family is surnamed Li (李), the Crown Prince is named 李承乾 (the actual name of Tang Taizong's deposed crown prince), and the Second Prince's arc parallels Li Tai (李泰). So the Qing court mirrors the Tang Taizong court. (Cited as a popular reader theory, *not* an authorial statement.)
- **Connection to character arc:** The deepest irony of the book: a state named "Celebration / Surplus Blessing" ruled by a cold, calculating emperor who murdered the very woman (Ye Qingmei) whose kindness should have generated that surplus blessing. The novel argues the surplus blessing skipped him and landed on his unacknowledged son.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 庆帝, 庆余年; Zhihu thread on the 留余庆 reference; Sohu article on the Tang Taizong parallel; The Paper feature on Qing-state naming.

## Character 5: Chen Pingping (陈萍萍)

- **Chinese:** 陈萍萍
- **Pinyin:** Chén Píngpíng
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 陈 (chén): Common Chinese surname (one of the top five). Also "to arrange, to display, to state." Classical: also means "old, aged, stale" (as in 陈酒 chénjiǔ "aged wine").
  - 萍 (píng): "Duckweed" — the floating green water-plant of classical Chinese poetry, the canonical symbol of a drifting, rootless life.
  - 萍 (repeated for affection / cuteness): The doubled-character (叠字 diézì) construction is conventionally feminine — actor 吴刚 has joked publicly he thought the role would require him to cross-dress when he first heard the name.
- **Combined name meaning:** "Old Duckweed-duckweed Chen." On the surface: a soft, almost feminine name for a maimed eunuch. Underneath: "rootless drifting" applied to a man who has lost everything.
- **Literary references:** The locus classicus is Wen Tianxiang's (文天祥) line in 《过零丁洋》: "山河破碎风飘絮，身世浮沉雨打萍 — Mountains and rivers shattered, like willow-down on the wind; my life rises and falls, like duckweed beaten by rain." 萍 = the floating-rootless self. Mao Ni's double-萍 explicitly invokes this register: Chen Pingping is a man whose life and country have been beaten by rain.
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Cited as in-novel backstory:** Chen Pingping's original name was 陈五常 Chén Wǔcháng (a generic male name). After he met Ye Qingmei, who treated him — a crippled eunuch — as an equal, he took the new name 陈萍萍 in her honor / to mark a new self.
  - **Common interpretation:** The doubled 萍 = (a) duckweed-drifting (his physical and political condition as a eunuch who is also the most feared spymaster in Qing); (b) feminine register that signals Ye Qingmei's gift of "equal treatment" — she treated him without the contempt the world reserved for castrated men; (c) a name that, in its softness, masks the most ruthless man in the empire.
- **Connection to character arc:** Pingping is the duckweed: politically rootless (a eunuch, technically powerless), but he has rooted his whole life in Ye Qingmei's memory and her son. The name's softness is the cover; the man underneath has been planning revenge against the Qing Emperor for thirty years.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 陈萍萍; People.cn HK feature on the character; multiple Zhihu/Sohu analyses.

## Character 6: Wang Qinian (王启年)

- **Chinese:** 王启年
- **Pinyin:** Wáng Qǐnián
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 王 (wáng): "King." The most common Chinese surname.
  - 启 (qǐ): "To open, to begin, to enlighten, to inform." Classical: as in 启蒙 qǐméng "enlightenment."
  - 年 (nián): "Year."
- **Combined name meaning:** "Wang who opens the year" / "Wang who begins the year." Literally a name of fresh beginnings.
- **Literary references / homophone:** Note that 启年 sounds similar to (but is not identical to) 祈年 qínián "praying for [a good] year" (as in 祈年殿 Qíniándiàn, the Hall of Prayer for Good Harvests in Beijing's Temple of Heaven). Zhihu commentators explicitly note "this is *not* a 祈年殿 reference, despite the resemblance."
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Author-stated (well-known among Qidian fans):** Around 2007, when 《庆余年》 was being serialized on Qidian, Mao Ni hosted a QQ-group meet-up. A real-life netizen with the screen-name "王启年" coordinated the group and threw the welcome dinner. Mao Ni promptly named a recurring side-character after him as an in-joke / thank-you. The fictional Wang Qinian is therefore an Easter-egg homage to the web-novel community, not a name with literary intent.
  - **Fan-extension joke:** His daughter is named 王霸 Wáng Bà ("King Tyrant"), with the doted nickname 霸霸 bàba — which sounds *exactly* like 爸爸 bàba "daddy." So whenever anyone calls the little girl by her cute nickname, they appear to be calling Wang Qinian "daddy." Mao Ni is openly trolling.
- **Connection to character arc:** Wang Qinian is the bumbling, money-pinching, comic-relief subordinate who turns out to be a high-stage martial artist. The name's "opening / beginning year" tracks with his role as the comic everyman who's been there since the start.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 王启年; Tencent News article on naming puns; Nanfang Metropolis Daily piece "龙套王 谈龙套的自我修养" (interview with the original real-life Wang Qinian); Zhihu Q&A 361452639.

## Character 7: Fan Sizhe (范思辙)

- **Chinese:** 范思辙
- **Pinyin:** Fàn Sīzhé
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 范 (fàn): Surname (as above).
  - 思 (sī): "To think, to consider, to long for, thought."
  - 辙 (zhé): "Wheel rut, track of a cart wheel." Idiomatic 重蹈覆辙 chóngdǎo-fùzhé = "to repeat a disastrous course" (literally "step again into the overturned rut").
- **Combined name meaning:** "Fan who thinks about the wheel-track" — i.e., one who thinks about the path / the cycle.
- **Literary references / homophone — this is the joke:**
  - 范思辙 is a near-perfect homophone of the Italian luxury fashion house 范思哲 (Fànsīzhé) — Versace. In the original web-novel he was reportedly named outright 范思哲 (Versace); the change of the final character from 哲 (zhé "wise") to 辙 (zhé "rut") is a face-saving spelling that keeps the pronunciation joke intact.
  - There is a separate, more literary reading: 苏辙 Sū Zhé was the Northern Song dynasty essayist (younger brother of Su Shi 苏轼), one of the *Three Sus* — i.e. 辙 is a recognizable hanzi from a serious literary name. Some fans take 范思辙 as "Fan thinks of Su Zhe" — a learning-aspiration name.
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Author-stated (Mao Ni public comments):** he has admitted online that the Fan-family names — 范建 (sounds like 犯贱 fànjiàn "shameless"), 范思辙 (Versace), 范若若 (his sister, with the reduplicated 若若) — are deliberate jokes at his own naming style after readers mocked him. He leans in.
  - **Common interpretation:** The Versace pun fits Fan Sizhe's defining trait: he is obsessed with money and turns out to be a commercial genius who ends up *fabulously* rich.
- **Connection to character arc:** A boy whose name puns on a luxury fashion brand turns out to be a born merchant. The cart-rut character (辙) is also a sneaky meta-joke — he keeps falling into the same rut (greed, schemes, getting himself in trouble) — but his economic instincts eventually make him one of the richest men in the empire.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 范思辙; Sina K article 庆余年：范思哲，这名字起得有水平!; Sohu article on Fan Sizhe's arc; Tencent News 谐音梗 piece.

## Character 8: Ye Qingmei (叶轻眉)

- **Chinese:** 叶轻眉
- **Pinyin:** Yè Qīngméi
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 叶 (yè): "Leaf." Common surname.
  - 轻 (qīng): "Light (in weight); slight; to take lightly; to disdain." Verb sense: 轻 + object = "to disdain / hold in contempt."
  - 眉 (méi): "Eyebrow." In classical Chinese, 须眉 xūméi ("beard and eyebrow") is the metonym for *men* (because only men have beards). 巾帼不让须眉 jīnguó-bùràng-xūméi = "the headscarf [woman] does not yield to beard-and-eyebrow [man]" = a woman the equal of any man.
- **Combined name meaning:** Literally "Ye Light-Eyebrow," but parsed against the idiom: **"Ye who looks down on [all the men of the world] / Ye who disdains beard-and-eyebrow."** The name is grammatically constructed as a manifesto.
- **Literary references:** The 须眉 / 巾帼 paired idiom is the entire classical vocabulary for "women equal to men." Naming her 轻眉 — "[I] disdain men [as a class]" — is a one-shot programmatic statement. This is the single most-cited "deliberate poetic naming" in the entire novel.
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Author-stated / community-consensus:** Ye Qingmei is a modern female engineering PhD who time-traveled to a quasi-medieval world. The name 轻眉 = "I look lightly upon men" encodes her feminist, equality-driven politics: she founded the Overwatch Council and the Inner Treasury, brought gunpowder, soap, glass, and democratic ideals to the Qing court, and pursued universal equality before being murdered.
  - **Universally cited interpretation** (Baidu Baike, Zhihu, Tencent News): "叶轻眉的名字意为看轻天下须眉" — "Ye Qingmei's name means 'to disdain all the men of the world.'"
- **Connection to character arc:** Her entire posthumous influence is what the *余庆* "surplus blessing" of the title refers to: her kindness and ideas keep saving her son and reshaping the empire long after she is dead.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 叶轻眉; Zhihu deep-dive 《庆余年》深度解析（叶轻眉前传）; Qidian 叶轻眉 character page; People.cn HK feature.

## Character 9: Haitang Duoduo (海棠朵朵)

- **Chinese:** 海棠朵朵
- **Pinyin:** Hǎitáng Duǒduǒ
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 海棠 (hǎitáng): "Chinese flowering crabapple / begonia." A canonical flower of classical Chinese poetry, ranked alongside plum, peach, and peony. (Two-character family name in this case — see below.)
  - 朵 (duǒ): "[Classifier for flowers/clouds]" — also "a blossom, a bloom."
  - 朵 (repeated): Reduplication = "blossom upon blossom," and the soft feminine register Mao Ni reaches for repeatedly (萍萍, 若若, 朵朵).
- **Combined name meaning:** "Crabapple, blossom-on-blossom." A botanical, feminine, poetic name.
- **Literary references — this is one of Mao Ni's richest:**
  - Su Shi's (苏轼) "Crabapple" 《海棠》: "东风袅袅泛崇光，香雾空蒙月转廊。只恐夜深花睡去，故烧高烵照红妆 — The east wind drifts gently, light shimmers; fragrant mist diffuses as the moon rounds the corridor. Fearing only that, deep at night, the flower will fall asleep, I burn the tall candle high to light her rouged face." The 海棠 is the canonical symbol of an unattainably beautiful, slightly melancholy female figure.
  - Li Qingzhao's (李清照) 《如梦令》: "知否知否，应是绿肥红瘦 — Don't you know, don't you know? It should be green grown plump, red grown lean." And the famous line 海棠依旧 ("the crabapple yet remains") — used as the title of fan essays about Haitang Duoduo's arc (e.g., the TapTap fan piece "他年安之若相问，只道海棠依旧").
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Common interpretation:** Haitang is the "blossom-on-blossom" — bucolic, peaceful, the only woman in the novel whose worldview fully matches Fan Xian's. Her name is the soft pastoral counterweight to the imperial intrigue. (Her original / birth-tribe name is reportedly 松芝仙令, Sōngzhī Xiānlìng — far harsher; "Haitang Duoduo" is the name she takes from her master Kuhe.)
  - **Fan-noted pun on the closing line of the series:** "他年安之若相问，只道海棠依旧 — If in some future year [Fan] Anzhi should ask, just say the crabapple remains as ever." Mao Ni's choice of 海棠 sets up this poetic farewell.
- **Connection to character arc:** She is the Northern Qi saintess who walks away from Fan Xian to return to the grasslands. Her name *is* the closing image of her arc — a perpetual blossom that endures regardless of empire or war.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 海棠朵朵 (multiple entries); TapTap fan analysis "他年安之若相问，只道海棠依旧"; Zhihu thread on Haitang and Fan Xian; Baidu Baike 海棠 (Su Shi poem entry).

## Character 10: Si Lili (司理理)

- **Chinese:** 司理理
- **Pinyin:** Sī Lǐlǐ
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 司 (sī): "To take charge of, to manage." Common in official titles (司令 sīlìng "commander"). Also a surname.
  - 理 (lǐ): "Reason, principle, logic, to arrange, to manage."
  - 理 (repeated): Mao Ni's reduplication signature.
- **Combined name meaning:** "Sī who manages reason / arranges principles." A name that, on paper, sounds bureaucratic.
- **Literary references — this is the key one:**
  - **司理理 ≈ 李师师 (Lǐ Shīshī).** Li Shishi was the most famous courtesan of the late Northern Song dynasty, allegedly the lover of Emperor Huizong (宋徽宗) and a recurring figure in 《水浒传》 (*Water Margin*). Reading "司理理" out loud and shifting the tones gets you very close to "李师师" — and 司理理's in-novel role is *also* the top courtesan of the Drunken Immortal House (醉仙居) in the Qing capital, doubled as an undercover Northern Qi spy.
  - The historical Li Shishi was also a spy-adjacent figure in folklore: she allegedly hid the poet Zhou Bangyan (周邦彦) under her bed when Emperor Huizong came visiting.
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Common interpretation (cited widely across Zhihu, Qidian, Baidu Baike):** Si Lili is Mao Ni's deliberate homophone-portrait of Li Shishi — top courtesan + political intrigue + tangled with the throne.
  - **Fan reading of the 司:** Some fans argue 司 ("to manage / take charge") plus doubled 理 ("reason / order") points to her actual function in the plot: she *manages* the intelligence flow between her clients. In other words, she is a "bureau of reason," literally a spy hub.
  - Her birth name in the novel is reportedly 李离思 Lǐ Lísī ("Li who thinks of departure") — a third layer of name-as-fate, since she is constantly torn between identities.
- **Connection to character arc:** She is a courtesan whose body is a tool of state intelligence. The name's surface ("manager of reason") is the cover; the homophone (李师师) is the historical reference; the underlying reality is a woman traded between empires.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 司理理; Baidu Baike 李师师; Wikipedia 李师师; Zhihu Q&A 361452639; Douban review of her arc.

## Character 11: Second Prince — Li Chengze (二皇子 李承泽)

- **Chinese:** 李承泽
- **Pinyin:** Lǐ Chéngzé
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 李 (lǐ): Surname. The imperial surname of the Tang dynasty — the deliberate parallel to the real historical Tang court is one of the novel's structural jokes.
  - 承 (chéng): "To inherit, to receive, to carry on, to bear."
  - 泽 (zé): "Marsh, lake; favor, grace, bounty, beneficent influence." Classical compound 恩泽 ēnzé = "imperial favor / grace conferred."
- **Combined name meaning:** "Li who inherits [imperial] grace." A perfect imperial-prince name — entitled by construction to receive the throne's bounty.
- **Literary references:**
  - The novel directly invokes the Tang Taizong (李世民) court. The historical parallel for 李承泽 is widely read by fans as **李泰 Li Tai**, Taizong's second son, who plotted against his brother and was disgraced.
  - 恩泽 / 承泽 are stock phrases in imperial-decree language.
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Fan-theorized (popular reading, not author-stated):** the prince literally named "Inheriting Grace" is the prince who *cannot* inherit — because he's a 磨刀石 módāoshí "whetstone" used by the Qing Emperor to sharpen the Crown Prince. The cruel irony is in the name.
  - **Drama-version observation:** The Qing Emperor uses both princes as tools. Both die with the throne never theirs to inherit.
- **Connection to character arc:** Li Chengze is the witty, cultured, doomed antagonist — a man whose entire identity is built on "inheriting grace" he will never receive. He commits suicide; his arc is a tragedy of misnamed expectations.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 李承泽; The Paper feature on Qing princes; Sohu article on the Tang Taizong parallel.

## Character 12: Crown Prince — Li Chengqian (太子 李承乾)

- **Chinese:** 李承乾 (novel) / 李承诚虔 in some drama-version reworkings (see note)
- **Pinyin:** Lǐ Chéngqián
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 李 (lǐ): As above.
  - 承 (chéng): "To inherit, to receive, to carry on."
  - 乾 (qián): The first hexagram of the *I Ching*, representing Heaven, the supreme yang, the emperor / father / leader principle. Compound 乾坤 qiánkūn = "Heaven and Earth, the cosmos." The single most "imperial" hanzi in the Chinese language.
- **Combined name meaning:** "Li who inherits Heaven / the throne." A name that explicitly claims succession to the highest cosmic principle.
- **Literary references:**
  - **The structural Tang parallel:** 李承乾 is the *actual historical name* of Tang Taizong's (李世民) original crown prince, who was deposed in 643 CE after a botched coup attempt against his father — the canonical Chinese-history cautionary tale of a crown prince undone by pressure and rivalry with a brother (李泰 — parallel to 李承泽 above). Mao Ni's reuse of the exact historical name is not coincidence; it is structural foreshadowing.
  - The 《周易·乾卦》 ("Hexagram Qian") symbolism: 乾 is also "the dragon" — the symbol of the emperor.
- **Note on drama spelling:** Some drama-version writeups note the show altered the characters to 李承诚虔 (changing 承乾 to 承诚虔) to avoid an exact match with the historical figure. Fan consensus is that the original novel uses 李承乾. (Flag: drama vs. novel spelling discrepancy — see Verification Flags.)
- **Author's intent / fan theories:**
  - **Strong fan-theorized / structurally obvious:** by reusing the exact historical name 李承乾, Mao Ni signals the Crown Prince's fate from page one — he will, like his Tang namesake, fail to inherit, be implicated in rebellion against his father, and be destroyed.
  - **Common interpretation:** The name "Inheriting Heaven" is hubris; the entire arc demonstrates that no one inherits Heaven by birthright.
- **Connection to character arc:** Named to inherit the cosmos; ends up dead, having never sat the throne. His pairing with brother 李承泽 (Inheriting Grace) is one of the novel's most pointed structural ironies — two brothers named for inheritance, neither inheriting anything.
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 李承乾 (Qing Yu Nian entries — multiple); 163.com article on Tang Taizong parallel; Sohu article 庆余年中的历史人物.

## Bonus: The Author's Pen Name — Mao Ni (猫腻)

- **Chinese:** 猫腻
- **Pinyin:** Māonì (sometimes Máonì in colloquial Beijing speech)
- **Character-by-character:**
  - 猫 (māo): "Cat."
  - 腻 (nì): "Greasy, oily; sick of, weary of; sticky, fine-grained."
- **Combined meaning:** As a fixed colloquial compound, 猫腻 means **"shady dealings, fishy business, a hidden trick, something not above-board."** A widely used Mandarin slang term (originally northern Mandarin / Beijing dialect). The metaphor: a cat covers its waste with sand to hide it — therefore 猫腻 = "what the cat is hiding."
- **Author's stated origin of the pen name:** Mao Ni has explained publicly that he chose the name because of exactly this idiomatic sense — "things not meant to be seen, hidden operations." It is a self-deprecating choice for a man who would go on to write some of the most morally and politically tangled web novels of the 2000s and 2010s.
- **Other naming notes about Mao Ni:**
  - Real name: 晓峰 Xiāo Fēng. Born 1977 in Yichang, Hubei.
  - First pen name: 北洋鼠 Běiyángshǔ ("Northern Ocean Mouse"), used in 2003 for his debut《映秀十年事》on Qidian.
  - Switched to 猫腻 for 《朱雀记》, then 《庆余年》 (2007–2009), 《间客》, 《将夜》, and 《大道朝天》.
- **Why it matters for this article:** Mao Ni's own pen name is itself a homophone-and-meaning game — exactly the kind of naming logic he applies to his characters. The author named "Shady Cat Business" is the man who named one prince "Inheriting Grace" (and let him die) and one spy "Speech / Ice / Cloud" (and made him incapable of warmth).
- **Sources:** Baidu Baike 猫腻; Wikipedia 猫腻 (网络作家); 中国作家网 / 广东作家网 feature; Zhihu 特别专题 piece on Mao Ni.

**Flagged for caveat / version-discrepancy:**

- **李承乾 vs. 李承诚虔 (Crown Prince).** The novel-version spelling 李承乾 is verified across multiple Baidu Baike entries and Zhihu discussions, and is the historically loaded one (matches Tang Taizong's deposed heir). However, some drama-related writeups state the TV adaptation altered the second and third hanzi to 承诚虔 to avoid a direct historical match. I have not been able to verify the drama-version spelling against a Tencent Video / official Maoyan source within this research pass. **Recommendation:** in the published article, use 李承乾 (novel canon) and add a parenthetical note about the possible drama-side respelling.

- **庆 = 唐 + 太 hanzi-decomposition theory.** Multiple Chinese popular-reading articles (163.com, Sohu) propose that 庆 is built from pieces of 唐 (Tang) + 太 (Taizong) — i.e. that the Qing state literally encodes "Tang Taizong" in its name. This is a striking fan reading but is *not* a Mao Ni statement and the hanzi-decomposition is loose (the components don't literally combine in standard 六书 etymology). **Label this clearly as "fan theory" in any published article.** What *is* well-established is that the Qing court mirrors the Tang Taizong court structurally (李 surname, 李承乾 crown prince, 李泰-parallel second prince).

- **言冰云 origin as a reader suggestion.** Multiple sources state the name was suggested by a reader and adopted by Mao Ni, who joked the name would otherwise have been 言冰冰 or 言云云. This is repeated consistently across Qidian Q&As and Zhihu but I did not locate the original Mao Ni quote / forum post directly. **Confidence: high but not primary-sourced — label as "widely reported, originally from web-serialization-era community."**

- **范闲 字 安之 granted "by 庆帝."** Multiple Qidian Q&A pages state this. Some Zhihu commenters argue the 字 actually originated with his mother / 范建. The "granted by the Qing Emperor" reading is the more commonly cited one but is contested in some readings. **Recommendation:** phrase as "his courtesy name 安之 is associated with the Qing Emperor's wish for him to live quietly" rather than claiming definitive in-novel attribution.

- **海棠朵朵 birth name 松芝仙令.** Cited in one search summary; not cross-verified in the primary fan wikis I reached. **Label as "fan-cited; verify against original novel chapters before publishing."**

- **司理理 birth name 李离思.** Same caveat — cited in one source but not deeply cross-confirmed. **Verify against novel text.**
