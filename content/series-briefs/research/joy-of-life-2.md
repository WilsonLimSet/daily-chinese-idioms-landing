# Joy of Life 2 (庆余年2) — Research Brief

## Quick facts

- **Chinese title:** 庆余年第二季 (Qìng Yú Nián Dì Èr Jì) — also widely written 庆余年2

Added to the National Library of China in 2020 and British Library in Nov 2024 — a rare honor for web fiction that marks Mao Ni as a foundational figure in the Chinese web-fiction canon alongside authors like Tianxia Bachang and Jiang Nan.
- **Premiere:** May 16, 2024 on Tencent Video (China) and Disney+ (select international markets including Singapore, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Malaysia, Indonesia; later Japan) — day-and-date. Ran through May 30, 2024.
- **Episodes:** 36
- **Director:** Sun Hao (孙皓) — returning from S1
- **Screenwriter:** Wang Juan (王倦) — returning from S1

  - Li Qin (李沁) as Lin Wan'er (林婉儿) — Fan Xian's wife, daughter of the Eldest Princess

## Plot (S1 recap + S2 premise, no S2 spoilers past ep 10)

**Framing device (novel only, censored in show):** A modern-day graduate student, Zhang Qingnuo, presents a thesis arguing that modern values (individual rights, equality under law, rule by reason) can only be illustrated by dropping a modern mind into an ancient world. What he writes becomes the story of Fan Xian. In the TV adaptation this frame is reduced to a Cold Open where Zhang Ruoyun's "student" narrates — China's regulatory climate restricts time-travel plots, so the show deliberately blurs whether Fan Xian literally has modern memories or is simply writing a thesis.

**S1 backbone:** Fan Xian is the illegitimate son of Finance Minister Fan Jian (范建) and Ye Qingmei (叶轻眉) — a mysterious genius (implied modern transplant) who founded the Overwatch Council and was assassinated. Raised in rural Danzhou by his grandmother and trained by the blind swordsman Wu Zhu (五竹), he arrives in the capital Jingdu, navigates marriage politics (engaged to Lin Wan'er), survives assassination attempts by the Eldest Princess's faction, and earns the Emperor's eye by demolishing a rival scholar at a Northern Qi banquet with the drunken poetry recital. S1 ends with Fan Xian's faked death after a Northern Qi mission.

**S2 premise (eps 1–10, safe):** Fan Xian returns from "death" to find the Second Prince's faction has moved against him, the Bao Yue Lou brothel network has been used to frame him, and the Overwatch Council — his power base — is under political attack from the Censorate (御史台). He must clear his name in open court, outmaneuver factions hungry to see him fall, negotiate his mother's institutional legacy, and reckon with the fact that the Emperor (eventually revealed as his biological father) is using him, Chen Pingping, and the Second Prince as pieces in a cold long game. Court intrigue, legal-trial structure, and political philosophy dominate over action.

## Cultural context: Chinese bureaucracy, poetry, philosophy

**Setting:** A fictional amalgam — the Qing Kingdom (庆国) is *not* the Qing Dynasty (清朝). Aesthetically it borrows Tang/Song literary culture, Ming court structure, and late-imperial bureaucratic offices. Three-state system: Southern Qing (庆), Northern Qi (北齐), Dong Yi City (东夷城). This "fake history" device lets the show reference real culture without provoking censors over specific dynasties.

**The Censorate (御史台):** A *real* Chinese institution dating to the Qin and Han, formalized under Tang. Censors had the extraordinary right to impeach ministers and even criticize the Emperor. In S2, the Censorate's clash with Fan Xian's Overwatch Council is the central political conflict, mapping onto the historic tension between Confucian moralist bureaucracy (censors) and imperial secret-intelligence apparatus (like the Ming 锦衣卫 Jinyiwei or Eastern Depot).

**The Overwatch Council (鉴查院 Jiàn Chá Yuàn):** Fictional, but clearly modeled on Ming-dynasty Jinyiwei/Dongchang. Eight bureaus cover surveillance, intelligence, weapons R&D, printing/censorship review, torture, assassination, and protection. Founded by Ye Qingmei to "watch over the Emperor" — a radical inversion of imperial prerogative that explains why the Emperor both needs and resents it.

**Poetry culture:** The scholar-official examination system required mastery of classical poetry. Being unable to compose or quote poems disqualified you from polite society — so a man who could recite hundreds of masterpieces at a banquet was, by definition, a civilizational titan. The modern Chinese "诗词大会" (Classical Poetry Competition, CCTV since 2016) revived this tradition and primed Chinese audiences to read Fan Xian's scene as a cultural triumph, not a stunt.

**Philosophical threads:** The show quotes or alludes to Confucian classics (《论语》, 《孟子》), Daoist texts (《道德经》), Legalist statecraft (implicit in Chen Pingping and the Emperor's ruthlessness), and the poetic/political philosophy of scholar-officials like Su Shi and Wang Anshi. Fan Xian's egalitarian outbursts are coded as Enlightenment-modern but dressed in classical Chinese phrasing.

## The iconic poetry recital scene (deep dive)

**Location in S1:** Episode 27, not S2. It's the most important single scene in the franchise and the cultural anchor for any Joy of Life 2 article.

**Setup:** Emperor hosts a banquet for diplomats from Northern Qi (led by aging literary grandmaster Zhuang Mohan 庄墨韩) and Dong Yi City. Zhuang publicly accuses Fan Xian of plagiarizing a poem from Zhuang's dead teacher. Fan Xian's whole literary reputation is at stake; the Emperor is watching.

**The performance:** Fan Xian grabs a wine jug, gets (actually) drunk, and in roughly four minutes delivers lines from between 24 and 38 classical poems spanning Han to Song dynasties. Actor Zhang Ruoyun reportedly drank real wine and memorized 100+ poems for the take.

**Confirmed poems quoted (partial list):**
- 《将进酒》 Jiāng Jìn Jiǔ — Li Bai (李白, Tang). "Bring in the Wine." "天生我材必有用" (Heaven made me with a purpose).
- 《虞美人》 Yú Měi Rén — Li Yu (李煜, Southern Tang). "春花秋月何时了" (When will spring flowers and autumn moon end?). Li Yu was a deposed emperor; poignant choice.
- 《水调歌头》 Shuǐ Diào Gē Tóu — Su Shi (苏轼, Song). "明月几时有" (When did the bright moon first appear?). Universally beloved Mid-Autumn poem.
- 《破阵子》 Pò Zhèn Zǐ — Xin Qiji (辛弃疾, Southern Song). "醉里挑灯看剑" (Drunk, I raised the lamp to examine my sword).
- 《长恨歌》 Cháng Hèn Gē — Bai Juyi (白居易, Tang). "Song of Everlasting Regret."
- 《别董大》 Bié Dǒng Dà — Gao Shi (高适, Tang). "莫愁前路无知己" (Don't fear the road ahead has no friend).
- 《梅花》 Méi Huā — Wang Anshi (王安石, Song).
- 《春望》 Chūn Wàng — Du Fu (杜甫, Tang). "国破山河在" (The state is broken, mountains and rivers remain).
- 《无题》 Wú Tí — Li Shangyin (李商隐, Tang).
- 《上邪》 Shàng Yé — anonymous Han yuefu.
- 《过零丁洋》 Guò Língdīng Yáng — Wen Tianxiang (文天祥, late Song). "人生自古谁无死" (Since ancient times who has not died?).
- 《登幽州台歌》 Dēng Yōuzhōu Tái Gē — Chen Ziang (陈子昂, Tang).
- 《山中与幽人对酌》 — Li Bai again.

**Cultural meaning:** The scene is essentially a civilizational flex. By quoting across dynasties — Han yuefu through Song ci — Fan Xian performs Chinese literary history as one seamless inheritance. The novel frames this as the modern Zhang Qingnuo "gifting" centuries of Chinese poetry to a world where those poems don't exist. For overseas Chinese and diaspora viewers, it hit as an emotional tribute to the classical canon. For international viewers on Disney+, it went viral as "the scene where a guy recites 300 poems drunk."

**Continuity to S2:** S2 references Fan Xian's reputation as "shige (诗仙) reborn" repeatedly; his poetic identity is now political leverage.

## Why Disney+ simultaneous release mattered

- **First day-and-date C-drama on Disney+.** Before this, Chinese dramas reached international streamers 2–3 weeks after domestic release, killing social-media momentum and hurting against live-subtitle Viki/YouTube competitors.
- **Deliberate partnership, not a pickup.** The Disney deal was negotiated *before* S2 started filming — Tencent/Disney agreed on simultaneous global distribution from the start, with Disney+ committing to outdoor ads and English-language marketing.
- **Territorial scope at launch:** Singapore, Hong Kong, Taiwan, Malaysia, Indonesia (Southeast Asia is Disney+'s strongest C-drama market). Later Japan, with multi-language dubs rolled out.
- **Outcome:** Most-watched mainland Chinese drama ever on Disney+. Global Times and Chinadaily reported 2–3x the viewership of competing top C-dramas internationally. Cracked mainstream English press (Deadline, Variety, Geek Culture covered the Disney+ move as a strategic inflection).
- **Aftermath:** Opened the door for subsequent titles like *The Legend of Shen Li* and *Blossoms Shanghai* to pursue similar international-simul deals. The industry shorthand "the Joy of Life model" now refers to coordinated Tencent–Disney+ global drops.

## Memorable quotes (15–20)

Quotes below are a mix of S1 and S2 lines widely circulated in Chinese fan communities. Translations are literal — some lose wordplay.

1. 一朝权在手，便把令来行。  
   *Yī zhāo quán zài shǒu, biàn bǎ lìng lái xíng.*  
   "The day power is in hand, is the day orders are given." — Fan Xian on taking charge of the Overwatch Council.

2. 这世上没有什么是一顿酒解决不了的，如果有，那就两顿。  
   *Zhè shìshàng méiyǒu shénme shì yī dùn jiǔ jiějué bùliǎo de, rúguǒ yǒu, nà jiù liǎng dùn.*  
   "There's nothing in this world a meal of wine can't solve. If there is, make it two." — Fan Xian, quintessential modern-mouth in ancient setting.

3. 我想让这天下百姓都能吃饱饭，穿暖衣，人人如龙。  
   *Wǒ xiǎng ràng zhè tiānxià bǎixìng dōu néng chī bǎo fàn, chuān nuǎn yī, rén rén rú lóng.*  
   "I want every commoner in this realm to eat their fill, wear warm clothes, and every person to stand like a dragon." — Fan Xian's mother Ye Qingmei's political credo, quoted by Fan Xian in S2.

4. 人人如龙。  
   *Rén rén rú lóng.*  
   "Every person a dragon." — Shorthand for Ye Qingmei's egalitarian dream; a S2 thematic refrain.

5. 若天不生我范闲，万古诗词如长夜。  
   *Ruò tiān bù shēng wǒ Fàn Xián, wàngǔ shīcí rú chángyè.*  
   "Had heaven not birthed me, Fan Xian, ten thousand ages of poetry would be one long night." — Fan Xian during the banquet scene (paraphrase of a Zhu Xi line about Confucius).

6. 天生我材必有用。  
   *Tiān shēng wǒ cái bì yǒu yòng.*  
   "Heaven gave me talent, it must have use." — Li Bai, recited by Fan Xian at the banquet.

7. 庆帝说：朕要的不是一个忠臣，朕要的是一把刀。  
   *Zhèn yào de bùshì yīgè zhōngchén, zhèn yào de shì yī bǎ dāo.*  
   "I do not want a loyal subject, I want a blade." — The Emperor on what he values in a servant.

8. 陈萍萍：我这一生，只信过两个人。  
   *Wǒ zhè yīshēng, zhǐ xìn guò liǎng gè rén.*  
   "In all my life, I have trusted only two people." — Chen Pingping (referring to Ye Qingmei and Fan Xian).

9. 我可以不救他，但我不能看着他死。  
   *Wǒ kěyǐ bù jiù tā, dàn wǒ bùnéng kànzhe tā sǐ.*  
   "I can choose not to save him, but I cannot watch him die." — Fan Xian.

10. 规矩是死的，人是活的。  
    *Guījǔ shì sǐ de, rén shì huó de.*  
    "Rules are dead things; people are alive." — Fan Xian, justifying bending procedure.

11. 读书人的事，能叫偷吗？  
    *Dúshū rén de shì, néng jiào tōu ma?*  
    "When a scholar does it, can you call it stealing?" — Fan Xian, comedic line adapted from Lu Xun's *Kong Yiji*.

12. 庆帝：这天下，是朕的。  
    *Zhè tiānxià, shì zhèn de.*  
    "This realm is mine." — The Emperor's standing claim.

13. 范闲：这天下，是天下人的。  
    *Zhè tiānxià, shì tiānxià rén de.*  
    "This realm belongs to the people of the realm." — Fan Xian's retort, echoing Mencius/Huang Zongxi.

14. 若有一日，我与父皇为敌，我亦不悔。  
    *Ruò yǒu yī rì, wǒ yǔ fùhuáng wéi dí, wǒ yì bù huǐ.*  
    "If one day I stand as enemy to my imperial father, I will not regret it." — Fan Xian (late S2 foreshadowing).

15. 人生如逆旅，我亦是行人。  
    *Rénshēng rú nìlǚ, wǒ yì shì xíngrén.*  
    "Life is an inn against the current; I too am a traveler." — Su Shi line Fan Xian quotes.

16. 我是谁的儿子不重要，重要的是我是谁。  
    *Wǒ shì shéi de érzi bù zhòngyào, zhòngyào de shì wǒ shì shéi.*  
    "Whose son I am doesn't matter; what matters is who I am." — Fan Xian confronting his paternity.

17. 二皇子：我输得起，但我不想输。  
    *Wǒ shū dé qǐ, dàn wǒ bù xiǎng shū.*  
    "I can afford to lose, but I don't want to." — Second Prince, defining his tragic arc.

18. 王启年：大人，又有刺客！  
    *Dàrén, yòu yǒu cìkè!*  
    "Sir — another assassin!" — Wang Qinian's recurring comic-relief line; a fan meme.

19. 你若要杀我，便来杀。你若要骗我，便骗到底。  
    *Nǐ ruò yào shā wǒ, biàn lái shā. Nǐ ruò yào piàn wǒ, biàn piàn dàodǐ.*  
    "If you would kill me, come and kill. If you would deceive me, deceive to the very end." — Fan Xian to Chen Pingping.

20. 我这一生，不过是借来的命。既然是借的，就要活得痛快。  
    *Wǒ zhè yīshēng, bùguò shì jiè lái de mìng. Jìrán shì jiè de, jiù yào huó dé tòngkuài.*  
    "My whole life is just a borrowed one. Since it's borrowed, I'll live it with gusto." — Fan Xian; nods to the hidden time-travel origin.

## Relevant idioms catalog (15–20)

Format: chengyu — pinyin — literal — figurative — Joy of Life 2 relevance.

1. **笑里藏刀** — *xiào lǐ cáng dāo* — "Hide a knife in a smile" — Friendly manners, murderous intent. Thirty-Six Stratagems #10. Originally describing Tang-era minister Li Yifu. *Relevance:* The Second Prince's entire court persona; also applies to the Eldest Princess.

2. **口蜜腹剑** — *kǒu mì fù jiàn* — "Honey in mouth, sword in belly" — Sweet words over hostile heart. From Tang minister Li Linfu. *Relevance:* Courtier behavior around the Emperor throughout S2.

3. **运筹帷幄** — *yùn chóu wéi wò* — "Plan strategy within the command tent" — Strategize from afar. Liu Bang's praise of Zhang Liang. *Relevance:* Chen Pingping's entire modus operandi — he fights from his wheelchair in the Overwatch Council.

4. **韬光养晦** — *tāo guāng yǎng huì* — "Hide brightness, nurture obscurity" — Conceal ability, bide time. *Relevance:* Fan Xian's strategy for years — acting the harmless poet-scholar while building networks.

5. **深谋远虑** — *shēn móu yuǎn lǜ* — "Deep plans, distant concerns" — Long-range strategic thinking. *Relevance:* The Emperor's decades-long plays against grandmasters and rival kingdoms.

6. **忍辱负重** — *rěn rǔ fù zhòng* — "Endure shame, bear heavy burden" — Swallow humiliation for a greater mission. *Relevance:* Chen Pingping's 20-year revenge setup for Ye Qingmei.

7. **一箭双雕** — *yī jiàn shuāng diāo* — "One arrow, two hawks" — Two goals with one move. *Relevance:* The Emperor's pattern of pitting son against son so both sharpen while he watches.

8. **螳螂捕蝉，黄雀在后** — *tángláng bǔ chán, huáng què zài hòu* — "Mantis stalks cicada, oriole waits behind" — While you target your prey, a bigger predator targets you. *Relevance:* The layered assassination plots in the first half of S2.

9. **卧虎藏龙** — *wò hǔ cáng lóng* — "Crouching tiger, hidden dragon" — Hidden talents. *Relevance:* The Overwatch Council is staffed with them; Wu Zhu being the literal hidden dragon.

10. **谋事在人，成事在天** — *móu shì zài rén, chéng shì zài tiān* — "Humans plan, heaven decides" — Effort vs. fate. *Relevance:* Fan Xian's repeated line after schemes partly fail.

11. **鞠躬尽瘁** — *jū gōng jìn cuì* — "Bow down, exhaust oneself" — Serve with total devotion until death. From Zhuge Liang. *Relevance:* Chen Pingping and Wang Qinian embody this toward Fan Xian / the throne.

12. **明哲保身** — *míng zhé bǎo shēn* — "Wise men protect themselves" — Avoid risk to stay safe. *Relevance:* The Censorate ministers and most court officials; contrasted with Yang Wanli and Fan Xian.

13. **一诺千金** — *yī nuò qiān jīn* — "One promise, a thousand gold" — A word is a bond. *Relevance:* Fan Xian's core honor principle — promises to his mother, to Wan'er, to the poor.

14. **为富不仁** — *wéi fù bù rén* — "Wealthy without benevolence" — Rich and ruthless. *Relevance:* The Ming merchant families Fan Xian takes down in S2 when seizing the internal treasury workshops.

15. **水落石出** — *shuǐ luò shí chū* — "When water recedes, stones appear" — Truth emerges in time. *Relevance:* The S2 trial arc structure — evidence slowly surfaces against Fan Xian's framers.

16. **大智若愚** — *dà zhì ruò yú* — "Great wisdom appears as foolishness" — Geniuses look simple. *Relevance:* Wang Qinian (the bumbling subordinate who's actually brilliant), Fan Sizhe (the money-obsessed brother who's a financial savant).

17. **众口铄金** — *zhòng kǒu shuò jīn* — "Many mouths melt metal" — Mass rumor destroys reputation. *Relevance:* The political weapon used against Fan Xian by the Censorate.

18. **临危不惧** — *lín wēi bù jù* — "Facing danger without fear" — Cool under threat. *Relevance:* Fan Xian's trial-scene demeanor.

19. **同归于尽** — *tóng guī yú jìn* — "Together return to extinction" — Mutual destruction. *Relevance:* A theme invoked between Fan Xian and Chen Pingping re: confronting the Emperor.

20. **舍生取义** — *shě shēng qǔ yì* — "Give up life to seize righteousness" — Die for principle. From Mencius. *Relevance:* Yang Wanli's speech to Fan Xian about being willing to die for justice — the emotional turning point of S2's middle arc.

## Fact-check flags

1. **Poem count in the banquet scene:** Sources disagree — some count 24 distinct poems, others 38, many fan posts claim "100" or "200" or "300." The 100+ figure appears to refer to how many Zhang Ruoyun memorized, not how many he recites on screen. For articles: say "over two dozen poems spanning Han through Song" or "Zhang Ruoyun memorized 100+ poems for the scene," and avoid stating a precise "200" without novel source verification.
2. **Disney+ territorial list:** Confirmed for Singapore, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Malaysia, Indonesia, later Japan. *Not* confirmed whether S2 streamed day-and-date in North America or Europe — Disney+ availability there is Hotstar-adjacent or regional. Do not claim "global Disney+ release" as if it included US/UK.
3. **"First C-drama on Disney+ day-and-date":** This framing comes from industry/fan press. Technically some prior titles had near-simultaneous Hotstar pickups. Safer claim: "the first major Chinese-mainland drama for which Tencent and Disney+ pre-negotiated synchronized international distribution" or "first headline-scale day-and-date C-drama on Disney+."
