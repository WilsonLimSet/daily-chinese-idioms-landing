## The 1990s Shanghai Economic Moment

The 1990s in Shanghai were the city's second great commercial reinvention, compressed into roughly a decade. After Mao's Cultural Revolution had hollowed out the city's pre-1949 cosmopolitan economy, two political events restarted the clock: the formal opening of Pudong in 1990 and Deng Xiaoping's Southern Tour (南巡, *nán xún*) in January–February 1992.

Deng's Southern Tour was a deliberate political intervention. By 1992 Deng held no formal Party position, having resigned the Central Military Commission chairmanship in 1989, yet he traveled to Shenzhen, Zhuhai, Guangzhou and finally Shanghai (arriving January 31, 1992; celebrating that Spring Festival in the city; touring Pudong's Nanpu Bridge on February 7) to publicly re-legitimize market reform after the post-Tiananmen freeze. Deng pushed specifically for opening Pudong, which State Council had announced on April 18, 1990 under Premier Li Peng but which had stalled politically ([Wikipedia: Deng Xiaoping's Southern Tour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping's_southern_tour); [Pudong - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pudong)).

The macroeconomic effect was immediate. GDP grew ~13% in both 1992 and 1993. Foreign direct investment leapt more than 150% year-on-year in 1992, reaching US$25 billion by 1993. Private ownership, which had operated in a legal gray zone since 1978, was for the first time given explicit political endorsement ([Wikipedia: Deng's Southern Tour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping's_southern_tour); [The Wire China: Jonathan Chatwin on the Southern Tour](https://www.thewirechina.com/2024/06/16/jonathan-chatwin-on-the-significance-of-deng-xiaopings-southern-tour/)).

The most visible cultural consequence was the **下海 (*xià hǎi*, "down to sea") wave**. In 1992 alone, more than 120,000 government officials resigned their iron-rice-bowl posts to enter private business, the second of three historical xiahai waves (the first was mid-to-late 1980s; the third in the 2000s) ([Sixth Tone: Why China's Bureaucrats Are Plunging Into the Private Sector](https://www.sixthtone.com/news/1003383); [Made in China Journal: Migration and the Globalisation of Chinese Capital](https://madeinchinajournal.com/2021/01/25/migration-and-the-globalisation-of-chinese-capital/)). The metaphor — "jumping into the sea" — captured both the danger (drowning) and the promise (open water) of leaving the state sector.

This is the world A-Bao (阿宝) inhabits in *Blossoms Shanghai*: a Shanghai where, in the early episodes, he is still a *xiaohai* (small ordinary kid); by the middle episodes he has become *Bao zǒng* (宝总, "President Bao"), an honorific reserved for businessmen with capital and influence.

## The Shanghai Stock Exchange and the 1990s Trading Boom

The Shanghai Stock Exchange (上海证券交易所) was formally established on November 26, 1990 and began trading operations on December 19, 1990 — the first organized stock exchange on the mainland since 1949. The original trading floor was rented space in the Astor House Hotel (浦江饭店) on the Bund, a heritage hotel near the Suzhou Creek that *Blossoms Shanghai* re-enacted on screen ([Wikipedia: Shanghai Stock Exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai_Stock_Exchange); [The China Project: The 1990 re-opening of Shanghai's stock exchange](https://thechinaproject.com/2022/12/21/something-old-something-new-the-1990-re-opening-of-shanghais-stock-exchange/); [SCMP: Wilting fortunes / Blossoms Shanghai](https://www.scmp.com/business/china-business/article/3249945/wilting-fortunes-wong-kar-wais-blossoms-shanghai-drama-series-offers-stock-investors-painful-reality)).

Demand massively outstripped supply. By 1992, the exchange listed only eight stocks — colloquially the **老八股 (*lǎo bā gǔ*, "Old Eight Stocks")** — all former state factories restructured as joint-stock companies ([Grokipedia: Shanghai Stock Exchange](https://grokipedia.com/page/Shanghai_Stock_Exchange); [Oreate AI: Thirty Years of China's Capital Market](https://www.oreateai.com/blog/thirty-years-of-chinas-capital-market-lessons-from-the-rise-and-fall-of-the-first-listed-companies/07d6d05f87fdeb5eb021a3c846c91373)). Together with five later issues, these thirteen tickers carry mythic status in Chinese financial history; trading any of them in 1990–1991 was effectively a license to print money.

To ration this scarcity, in 1992 Shanghai authorities adopted a **subscription-certificate lottery system (认购证, *rèn gòu zhèng*)**. Each ticket cost 30 yuan; a winning draw entitled the holder to buy a limited quantity of newly issued shares. Black-market resale of unredeemed certificates ran at 4,000%+ premiums (a 30-yuan certificate resold for 1,400 yuan, roughly US$280 at the time) ([The China Project](https://thechinaproject.com/2022/12/21/something-old-something-new-the-1990-re-opening-of-shanghais-stock-exchange/)).

The system's failure mode arrived in Shenzhen, not Shanghai, on **August 10, 1992** — the so-called **"8.10 incident" (8·10事件)**. Roughly 1 million people queued for application forms to Shenzhen Development Bank shares; only 500,000 shares were available; corrupt officials had sold most forms on the black market before queueing opened; rioting followed. The incident catalyzed the founding of the China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC) later that year ([Wikipedia: Deng's Southern Tour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping's_southern_tour); [The China Project](https://thechinaproject.com/2022/12/21/something-old-something-new-the-1990-re-opening-of-shanghais-stock-exchange/)).

The figure who personifies the era is **杨百万 (Yáng Bǎiwàn, "Millionaire Yang")** — birth name Yang Huaiding, a 40-year-old steel-factory warehouse keeper earning 51 yuan/month who in 1988–1990 made his first million yuan arbitraging treasury bonds between Shanghai and provincial cities (e.g., buying in Hefei, selling same-day in Shanghai), then bought 500 shares of Shanghai Electric Vacuum (one of the Old Eight) on the exchange's first trading day at 365.70 yuan, closing at 384 yuan. He went on to teach retail investors at his own Shanghai workshop and write trading books. He died in 2021 ([Yicai Global: Yang Huaiding obituary](https://www.yicaiglobal.com/news/yang-huaiding-china-first-stock-investor-dies-aged-71); [SCMP: 30 years of Millionaire Yang](https://www.scmp.com/business/markets/article/3114540/chinas-stock-exchanges-and-millionaire-yang-mark-30-years-pride)).

*Blossoms Shanghai* hired a veteran market analyst as consultant to ensure terminology and trading-floor accuracy ([SCMP: Wilting fortunes](https://www.scmp.com/business/china-business/article/3249945/wilting-fortunes-wong-kar-wais-blossoms-shanghai-drama-series-offers-stock-investors-painful-reality)).

## 8–10 Stock-Market Terms Featured in the Drama

Each term verified across at least two sources.

### 1. 大户 (*dàhù*) — "big player" / large-capital trader
Stereotypically the lone hero/anti-hero — "the cowboy of the Shanghai stock market." Sufficient capital to move prices in an Old-Eight-Stock-era thin market. *Blossoms Shanghai*'s Bao zǒng is essentially a *dàhù*. ([Cambridge / Hertz, *The Trading Crowd*, Ch. 6](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/trading-crowd/dispersed-players-sanhu/1A9804A543FD12AF2C327F3762F883E4); [bab.la dictionary](https://en.bab.la/dictionary/english-chinese/retail-investor))

### 2. 散户 (*sǎnhù*) — retail / "scattered" investor
The "散" literally means *loose, scattered, unorganized*. Sǎnhù are the crowd-manqué: numerous but lacking coherence as a group. China's stock market is famously sǎnhù-dominated to this day, a structural feature that began in 1990 ([Annual Reviews: Retail and Institutional Investor Trading in China](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-financial-082123-110132); [Cambridge / Hertz](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/trading-crowd/dispersed-players-sanhu/1A9804A543FD12AF2C327F3762F883E4)).

### 3. 老八股 (*lǎo bā gǔ*) — "the Old Eight Stocks"
The eight original A-share listings on the Shanghai exchange through 1992 (e.g., Shanghai Electric Vacuum, Yanzhong Industrial). The Old Eight have legendary status; owning them at IPO was the *Blossoms Shanghai* origin story for several characters. ([Grokipedia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Shanghai_Stock_Exchange); [Oreate AI](https://www.oreateai.com/blog/thirty-years-of-chinas-capital-market-lessons-from-the-rise-and-fall-of-the-first-listed-companies/07d6d05f87fdeb5eb021a3c846c91373))

### 4. 认购证 (*rèn gòu zhèng*) — "subscription certificate"
The 30-yuan lottery ticket that gave the holder the right to subscribe to new IPOs in 1992 Shanghai. Black-market premium routinely exceeded 4,000%. The certificate is the McGuffin behind multiple sub-plots in early-1990s Chinese financial history. ([The China Project](https://thechinaproject.com/2022/12/21/something-old-something-new-the-1990-re-opening-of-shanghais-stock-exchange/))

### 5. 原始股 (*yuán shǐ gǔ*) — "original stock" / pre-IPO shares
Shares issued before a company is publicly listed, typically at a sharp discount to expected market price. In 1990s Chinese popular usage, 原始股 became synonymous with "instant wealth." Securities regulations later imposed lock-up periods and capital-gains taxes. ([Cngold encyclopedia](http://stock.cngold.org/zt/yuanshigu.html); [Zhihu Q&A on yuanshigu](https://www.zhihu.com/question/68388163))

### 6. 327国债 (*sān èr qī guó zhài*) — "Series 327 Treasury Bond"
The single most infamous event in 1990s Shanghai trading. 327 was a futures contract on a 1992-issued 3-year treasury bill (RMB 240 bn face, ~9.5% coupon) maturing June 1995. On **February 23, 1995**, the Ministry of Finance announced redemption at 148.50 yuan, ruining short sellers. Wanguo Securities (万国证券), the dominant short, faced ~5.6 billion yuan in losses; in a panic at 4:22 pm it placed enormous illegal sell orders. The Shanghai Stock Exchange voided the final 8 minutes of trading at 10 pm. Treasury bond futures were suspended nationally on May 17, 1995 — and stayed suspended for 18 years. ([Ice Cream Tutor: "327" Treasury Bond Futures Incident](https://www.icecreamtutor.com/notes/management/crisis-management/327-treasury-bond-futures-incident/); [Oreate AI: Major Risk Events](https://www.oreateai.com/blog/major-risk-events-in-chinas-financial-history-analysis-of-the-327-government-bond-futures-case/b0b97c8770ca2969fa728db6399d5617); [Kapronasia: Treasury Bond Futures in China](https://www.kapronasia.com/china-capital-markets-research-category/the-future-of-treasury-bond-futures-in-china.html))

### 7. 涨停 / 跌停 (*zhǎng tíng* / *diē tíng*) — daily up-limit / down-limit
Maximum single-session price change (now ±10% for A-shares; the limit-system was introduced after early-1990s wild swings). 涨停 ("hit the up-limit") and 跌停 ("hit the down-limit") are visceral words in 1990s trading-floor vocabulary, denoting the most dramatic single-day moves a stock can make. ([Reverso Context: 涨停](https://context.reverso.net/translation/chinese-english/%E6%B6%A8%E5%81%9C); [Reverso Context: 跌停](https://context.reverso.net/translation/chinese-english/%E8%B7%8C%E5%81%9C))

### 8. 牛市 / 熊市 (*niú shì* / *xióng shì*) — bull market / bear market
Direct calques of the English. In 1992 Shanghai, the bull-run that followed Deng's Southern Tour drove the SSE composite from ~400 to ~1,500 in months; the subsequent bear-market crash wiped out swathes of retail investors. ([Zhihu: 牛熊市的八大法则](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/584261418); [POEMS: Stock market terms](https://www.poems.com.sg/zh-hans/market-journal/stock-market-terms-made-easy-understand-the-jargon-in-just-five-minutes/))

### 9. 庄家 (*zhuāng jiā*) — "house" / market manipulator
A large player (often a brokerage or syndicate) who accumulates a controlling block in a thinly-traded counter and then drives the price with coordinated trades. In the wild-west early 1990s Shanghai market with no real surveillance, 庄家 activity was widespread; the term remains pejorative. ([Zhihu: 牛熊市的八大法则](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/584261418))

### 10. 黄牛 (*huáng niú*) — "yellow ox" / scalper
Originally a 1920s–30s Shanghai term for rickshaw pullers with connections at train and bus stations who pocketed a surcharge for buying tickets. In the early 1990s the same word was applied to people who lined up to scalp share-subscription certificates on the black market. ([The World of Chinese: Yellow Oxen](https://www.theworldofchinese.com/2023/06/yellow-oxen-how-chinas-ticket-scalpers-keep-turning-a-profit/); [eChineseLearning: 黄牛党](https://www.echineselearning.com/blog/tickets-are-sold-out-better-find-a-huangniudang))

## The Huanghe Road Restaurant Scene (Real History + Drama Recreation)

**Huanghe Road (黄河路)** is a short, kinked street running off People's Square in central Shanghai — adjacent to the city government and only a short walk from the Shanghai Stock Exchange's first home on the Bund. In the 1990s it was Shanghai's most concentrated restaurant strip, with over 100 establishments, many serving expensive Cantonese and "Hong Kong-style" cuisine that catered to the new business class. Photographs of restaurant owners posing with visiting Hong Kong pop stars and film actors were standard décor ([english.shanghai.gov.cn: Former food haven blossoms once more](https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-Latest-WhatsNew/20240110/1ba02195e1ef4de49fabee498555c64d.html); [meet-in-shanghai.net: Street food for thought](https://www.meet-in-shanghai.net/en/guide/street-food-for-thought-traditional-shanghai-cuisine-blossoms-again-845072/); [China Daily: Former food haven](https://www.chinadaily.com.cn/a/202401/09/WS659d3b1ca3105f21a507b674_6.html)).

What mattered about Huanghe Road was not the cooking but the function: it was a **business arena**. As the China Daily / Shanghai Government tourism portal puts it, "Huanghe Road, adjacent to the stock exchange, was more than a food street; it was a colossal business arena where deals were made between clinking glasses." The peak years were 1993–1996.

The strip's decline began in the mid-1990s as new safety regulations forced removal of the iconic neon signs, an exotic-snake delicacy was banned, the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis collapsed demand, and the 2003 SARS epidemic put the final nail in many businesses. By the 2010s most of the original venues had closed ([english.shanghai.gov.cn](https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-Latest-WhatsNew/20240110/1ba02195e1ef4de49fabee498555c64d.html)).

**The fictional 至真园 (*Zhì zhēn yuán*, "Garden of Utmost Truth") in the drama is based on a real restaurant, 苔圣园 (*Tái shèng yuán*), which opened on Huanghe Road in 1993 and is still operating.** After *Blossoms Shanghai* aired, Tai Sheng Yuan saw online sales jump 170% in a week and group-purchase orders rise 240% ([China Daily: 《繁花》"点燃"上海黄河路](https://language.chinadaily.com.cn/a/202401/12/WS65a10963a3105f21a507c072.html); [english.shanghai.gov.cn: Blossoms Shanghai TV series sparks culinary craze](https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-Restaurants/20240111/40ed2ada0a184ee3afb32d13fa17bc85.html)).

Wong Kar-wai did not film on the actual street as it now stands. Most Huanghe Road exteriors and interiors in the drama were reconstructed on a 5,000+ sqm sound stage at Shanghai Film Park in Songjiang District's Chedun town, with neon signs, period menus and restaurant fittings recreated from period photographs and viewer submissions. Production solicited 1990s memorabilia from Shanghai residents via a *Xinmin Evening News* (新民晚报) appeal ([SCMP: 5 locations from Blossoms Shanghai](https://www.scmp.com/lifestyle/travel-leisure/article/3270604/5-locations-wong-kar-wais-blossoms-shanghai-are-now-tourist-hotspots); [Grokipedia: Blossoms Shanghai](https://grokipedia.com/page/Blossoms_Shanghai)).

After the drama's January 2024 success, Shanghai's municipal government restored Huanghe Road to its 1990s appearance with retrofitted neon signage; the street reopened as a tourism site in July 2024 ([english.shanghai.gov.cn: Restored Huanghe Road to reopen](https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-Latest-WhatsNew/20240710/35e3b6c4d4c64d099216ad4a551f81f1.html)).

Adjacent **Jinxian Road (进贤路)** — a quieter street with the historic 海金滋 (Haijinzi), 兰心 (Lanxin) and 茂隆 (Maolong) restaurants — was also a filming location and tourism magnet post-release ([SCMP: 5 locations](https://www.scmp.com/lifestyle/travel-leisure/article/3270604/5-locations-wong-kar-wais-blossoms-shanghai-are-now-tourist-hotspots); [Grokipedia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Blossoms_Shanghai)).

## "A-Bao" (阿宝) and the Shanghainese Naming/Business Culture

The prefix **阿 (*ā*)** before a person's given name (or the last syllable of it) is a Wu / southern Chinese diminutive used to express **familiarity, affection, or informality**. Outside Mandarin-speaking northern China, it is one of the dominant kinship and friendship address forms — A-Hua, A-Ming, A-Bao, A-Mao. It is used both by elders to juniors and between intimates of similar age. In a Shanghainese context, *阿* signals that the speaker is using the local register, not the official Mandarin one ([Wiktionary: 阿](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%98%BF); [Mandarin Blueprint: Chinese Prefixes and Suffixes](https://www.mandarinblueprint.com/blog/chinese-prefixes-and-suffixes/); [Keats School: Common Prefixes and Suffixes](https://keatschinese.com/china-culture-resources/lesson-8-elementary-common-prefixes-and-suffixes-in-chinese/)).

阿宝 (*Ā Bǎo*) literally reads "A-Treasure." 宝 (*bǎo*, "treasure, precious thing") is one of the most common diminutive-name elements in southern China — Chinese parents call infants 宝宝 or 小宝. So the name signals: a local Shanghainese (the *ā* prefix), called by his everyday familiar name (the 宝). It is a *deliberately unpretentious* name for a man who becomes hugely rich.

The arc of his address-form is itself the story. In early 1990s flashbacks he is **小宝** or **阿宝** — boy-Treasure. By the mid-1990s scenes he has become **宝总 (*Bǎo zǒng*)**, contracting *Bǎo zǒngjīnglǐ* ("General Manager Bao" / "President Bao"). The 总 suffix is the 1990s business-class status marker par excellence: it is what you call a man whose business card has *zǒngjīnglǐ* on it, or, looser, anyone who controls capital. The drama tracks A-Bao's transformation from neighbourhood kid to *Bao zǒng* as the central transformation of the period ([Wikipedia: Blossoms Shanghai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blossoms_Shanghai); [Criterion Channel](https://www.criterionchannel.com/blossoms-shanghai); [Sixth Tone: Wong Kar-wai's Love Letter](https://www.sixthtone.com/news/1014517)).

This linguistic shift — from kin-form to business-form — is a microcosm of Shanghai in the 1990s: the same person, two registers, separated by capital.

## Old Shanghai Social Codes — Face, Guanxi, Local vs Outsider

### 面子 (*miànzi*, "face")
Social capital, standing, prestige. In the *Blossoms Shanghai* dining culture, *miànzi* is the operative currency of every restaurant scene: who picks up the bill, who is seated where, who is greeted by name by the proprietress. Mianzi is reciprocal — to deny it (refuse a return favor, embarrass someone publicly) is to break a relationship ([World Bank IFC: Guanxi, Mianzi, and Business](https://www.ifc.org/en/insights-reports/2012/pso-26-cg-and-china); [Asia Media Centre: Chinese business values](https://www.asiamediacentre.org.nz/features/chinese-business-values-guanxi-mianzi-renqing)).

### 关系 (*guānxi*, "relationships")
Personal network ties grounded in Confucian reciprocity. In 1990s commerce, guanxi *was* business infrastructure — the substitute for the rule-of-law, contract-enforcement and price-discovery mechanisms that did not yet exist. Foreign business literature of the era often warned that "in the 1990s, guanxi was frequently disregarded for American business leaders when dealing with local officials" and that "corruption replaced guanxi in the form of tangible or monetary gifts," a shift the drama itself plays with via Miss Wang's Foreign Trade Office liaisons with Bao zǒng ([Wikipedia: Guanxi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanxi); [Asia Media Centre](https://www.asiamediacentre.org.nz/features/chinese-business-values-guanxi-mianzi-renqing)).

### 本地人 vs 外地人 (*běndì rén* vs *wàidì rén*, locals vs outsiders)
Between the late 1980s and mid-1990s, China's internal migrant population doubled from ~8.9 million (1989) to ~23 million (1994). Shanghai was a primary destination. Shanghai's first municipal migrant policy in 1991 was summarized in three keywords: **"containment, deportation, and management."** By the 2010 census, ~9 million of Shanghai's 23 million residents (almost 40%) were migrants without a Shanghai *hukou*. These "新上海人" ("New Shanghainese") were and are linguistically distinguished — they generally don't speak Shanghainese, only Mandarin ([Wikipedia: Shanghainese people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghainese_people); [China Perspectives: Migrant Workers in Shanghai](https://journals.openedition.org/chinaperspectives/456); [Wikipedia: Migration in China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Migration_in_China)).

The 本地/外地 line is not a polite social distinction in 1990s Shanghai — it is loaded with class and economic resentment. *Blossoms Shanghai* dramatizes this in casting and dialect: characters speaking Shanghainese (in the Shanghainese edit of the show) versus those who speak only Mandarin are marked as local or non-local. Approximately 1,000 uses of the Shanghainese word **不响 / 讷行 (*vehxiang* in romanization, meaning "to keep silent")** in the original novel make dialect-fluency itself a marker of belonging ([Sixth Tone: Shanghai on Screen](https://www.sixthtone.com/news/1014442); [Jin Yucheng Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jin_Yucheng); [Brill: Frontiers of Literary Studies in China review](https://brill.com/view/journals/flsc/10/4/article-p756_13.xml)).

### 上只角 vs 下只角 (*shàng zhī jiǎo* vs *xià zhī jiǎo*, "upper corner" vs "lower corner")
A specifically Shanghainese spatial-social classification of pre-1990s Shanghai. The "upper corner" (former French Concession, western International Settlement, the area around Huaihai Road) was where well-off Shanghainese and Westernized elites lived; the "lower corner" (Yangpu, Zhabei, Putuo, Baoshan — the industrial northeast) was where working-class migrants from Subei (northern Jiangsu), Shandong and Anhui settled. The line was the Suzhou Creek. The "lower corner" was famous for its 二房东 (sub-landlords) chopping houses into rooms with shared kitchens. Language reinforced the geography: upper-corner residents spoke standard Shanghainese (with Ningbo influence); lower-corner residents spoke Subei-accented Shanghainese ([上只角 - 百度百科](https://baike.baidu.com/item/%E4%B8%8A%E5%8F%AA%E8%A7%92/6386884); [Sina blog: 繁花里的上海帮派 上海的"上只角"和"下只角"](https://blog.sina.com.cn/s/blog_5ef1fe090102zrc0.html); [The Paper: 上海两区合并 / "上下只角"是文化潜意识](https://www.thepaper.cn/newsDetail_forward_1383289); [Sohu: 上只角与下只角](https://www.sohu.com/a/222076821_100110782)).

The categories are now considered antiquated — they made sense in a Shanghai that ended at the Huangpu River. Pudong's rise after 1990 reshuffled the whole map. But the 1990s setting of *Blossoms Shanghai* is precisely the moment when these inherited geographies were colliding with the new commercial geography of the stock exchange and Huanghe Road.

## Real Locations in Blossoms Shanghai

The drama was filmed in a combination of restored Bund-area landmarks and large soundstage reconstructions:

- **Fairmont Peace Hotel (和平饭店)** — The 1929 Art Deco hotel on the Bund, formerly the Cathay Hotel built by Victor Sassoon, served as Bao zǒng's residence and one of the drama's signature visual locations. Real filming took place inside. Post-release, the hotel saw a tourism surge ([Wikipedia: Peace Hotel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peace_Hotel); [WildChina: Fairmont Peace Hotel in the Blossoms Shanghai Era](https://wildchina.com/2024/02/the-fairmont-peace-hotel-in-the-blossoms-shanghai-era-and-beyond/); [SCMP: 5 locations](https://www.scmp.com/lifestyle/travel-leisure/article/3270604/5-locations-wong-kar-wais-blossoms-shanghai-are-now-tourist-hotspots)).
- **The Bund (外滩)** — The waterfront promenade and its early-20th-century European architecture appear in numerous exterior scenes. Wong Kar-wai used surviving facades rather than recreations where possible.
- **Astor House Hotel (浦江饭店)** — The original 1990 Shanghai Stock Exchange trading floor was rented space in this hotel's ballroom. The drama re-enacted those scenes ([SCMP: Wilting fortunes](https://www.scmp.com/business/china-business/article/3249945/wilting-fortunes-wong-kar-wais-blossoms-shanghai-drama-series-offers-stock-investors-painful-reality)).
- **ICBC Xikang Road branch (西康路工商银行)** — In the early 1990s, before formal brokerages were ubiquitous, retail investors traded shares through trading counters housed inside commercial bank branches. The drama recreated one such counter ([SCMP: Wilting fortunes](https://www.scmp.com/business/china-business/article/3249945/wilting-fortunes-wong-kar-wais-blossoms-shanghai-drama-series-offers-stock-investors-painful-reality)).
- **Jinjiang Hotel (锦江饭店)** — A historic hotel used for filming, in the former French Concession.
- **Fuzhou Road (福州路)** — A historic commercial street featured in filming ([english.shanghai.gov.cn: City walks](https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-CityTour/20240111/ae1c56c2ab5a4da590686a63cf0c59ea.html)).
- **Shanghai Film Park, Chedun, Songjiang District (松江车墩影视基地)** — The bulk of Huanghe Road and lane-house interiors were built on this 5,000+ sqm soundstage ([Grokipedia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Blossoms_Shanghai)).
- **Jinxian Road (进贤路)** — Quieter restaurant lane in the former French Concession; Haijinzi (海金滋), Lanxin (兰心), and Maolong (茂隆) were among historic eateries featured.

The Oriental Pearl Tower (东方明珠) appears in the background of multiple scenes, still under construction. It was completed in 1994 — itself a date-stamp on the era the drama depicts ([Lujiazui - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lujiazui); [Sixth Tone: Wong Kar-wai's Love Letter](https://www.sixthtone.com/news/1014517)).

## Period Vocabulary Now Faded

A glossary of Chinese terms that meant something specific in 1980s–90s commercial culture and have since lost currency or shifted meaning. These are the words that period-mark *Blossoms Shanghai* most precisely.

| Term | Pinyin | Literal | 1990s meaning | Status today |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 万元户 | *wàn yuán hù* | "ten-thousand-yuan household" | Late-1970s / 1980s wealth marker for rural and small-town entrepreneurs. 10,000 yuan was equivalent to roughly US$1,500 at the time, but represented an extraordinary household savings level in an era when most urban workers earned under 100 yuan/month. ([CGTN: Ten-thousand-yuan household](https://news.cgtn.com/news/77557a4d7a494464776c6d636a4e6e62684a4856/share_p.html); [Springer: Ten-Thousand-Yuán Households](https://link.springer.com/rwe/10.1007/978-981-97-9462-1_32)) | Archaic — Chinese GDP per capita is now ~80,000+ yuan annually. |
| 下海 | *xià hǎi* | "down to the sea" | To quit a state-sector job for private business. Peak usage 1992 (post-Southern Tour wave: 120,000+ officials resigned). ([Sixth Tone](https://www.sixthtone.com/news/1003383); [Made in China Journal](https://madeinchinajournal.com/2021/01/25/migration-and-the-globalisation-of-chinese-capital/)) | Largely retired — there is no longer the same iron-rice-bowl/private-sector binary. |
| 个体户 | *gè tǐ hù* | "individual household" | A self-employed small merchant or peddler. Originally pejorative in the early 1980s (connoting selfish money-grubbing); by the late 1980s a legitimate small-business category; later overtaken by 民营企业 (private enterprise) terminology. ([ChinaDaily: Deng Xiaoping opens a door for private economy](https://www.chinadaily.com.cn/china/2014-08/19/content_18449095.htm); [Far Outliers: getihu](https://faroutliers.com/2016/12/30/wordcatcher-tales-getihu-baochan-daohu/)) | Still in legal use but historically tinged. |
| 大哥大 | *dà gē dà* | "big-brother-big" | The brick mobile phone of 1990s China. The Motorola 8000-series imported into China from 1987; a Guangdong businessman paid 20,000 yuan for one in 1991 (US$3,745, at a time when monthly wages were under 500 yuan). Carrying one standing-upright on a Huanghe Road dinner table was a deliberate status display. ([CGTN: Chinese Terminology - Big Brother](https://news.cgtn.com/news/3d3d514e3341444d31457a6333566d54/index.html); [SCMP / Yahoo: How mobile phones in China have gone from status symbol](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/mobile-phones-china-gone-status-093000717.html)) | Obsolete — replaced by 手机 (*shǒujī*). |
| BP机 | *BP jī* | "BP machine" / pager | The numeric pager. China's first paging service launched in Shanghai in 1983; the city introduced Chinese-character paging in 1991; nationwide pager users peaked at 65 million in 1998. By 2002 pagers had largely been replaced by mobile phones. ([China Daily: The great beep backward](https://www.chinadaily.com.cn/cndy/2007-04/10/content_846652.htm)) | Obsolete. |
| 老克勒 | *lǎo kè lè* | "old clerk" / "old carat" (loanword) | Pre-1949 cosmopolitan Shanghainese gentleman who maintained the bilingual, jazz-listening, tailored-suit lifestyle of the foreign-concession era. The word is a Wu phonetic borrowing from English "clerk" or "carat." In 1990s Shanghai, surviving 老克勒 in their 60s–80s were objects of nostalgia. ([HiNative: 老克勒 vs 老炮儿](https://hinative.com/questions/1379694); [Travelofchina.com: Culture in Shanghai - Haipai Guide](https://www.travelofchina.com/culture-in-shanghai/)) | Still used, but as a heritage / nostalgia term, not a contemporary social category. |
| 总经理 / 总 (-zǒng) | *zǒng jīng lǐ / -zǒng* | "general manager" | A 1990s honorific for any man who runs a business. Bao zǒng (宝总) is the canonical 1990s usage. ([Wikipedia: Blossoms Shanghai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blossoms_Shanghai)) | Still ubiquitous in business address. |
| 认购证 | *rèn gòu zhèng* | "subscription certificate" | The 1992 Shanghai IPO lottery ticket. ([The China Project](https://thechinaproject.com/2022/12/21/something-old-something-new-the-1990-re-opening-of-shanghais-stock-exchange/)) | Obsolete — replaced by online subscription systems. |

## Verification Flags

Items the article writer should double-check or flag as approximations rather than hard facts:

1. **Tai Sheng Yuan / 苔圣园 as the Zhi Zhen Yuan model.** Multiple Chinese-language and Shanghai-government tourism sources confirm this identification, but the production has not (to my knowledge) explicitly named the inspiration. Treat as widely-reported but not officially confirmed by Wong Kar-wai. Sources: [china.daily.com.cn](https://language.chinadaily.com.cn/a/202401/12/WS65a10963a3105f21a507c072.html); [meet-in-shanghai.net](https://www.meet-in-shanghai.net/en/guide/street-food-for-thought-traditional-shanghai-cuisine-blossoms-again-845072/).

2. **"vehxiang" / 不响 Shanghainese transcription.** Sixth Tone gives the romanization as "vehxiang"; the Mandarin reading is *bù xiǎng* (不响, literally "not making a sound" → "to keep silent"). The Wu Chinese pronunciation differs significantly from Mandarin and there is no single canonical romanization standard; treat "vehxiang" as illustrative and use 不响 / *bù xiǎng* when writing.

3. **杨百万 / Yang Baiwan's exact 1990 trade.** Sources broadly agree he was an early SSE trader and bought one of the Old Eight (Shanghai Electric Vacuum) on Day 1 (December 19, 1990). Different sources give slightly different share quantities (500 shares is the figure SCMP cites); treat numerical detail as "approximate, varies by source."

4. **"327 incident" date and loss figures.** February 23, 1995 is firm and well-attested; Wanguo Securities' loss is given in some sources as RMB 5.6 billion, in others as US$718.5 million — these are not the same number at the 1995 exchange rate, and represent slightly different things (total exposure vs realized loss). The CSRC formally suspended treasury bond futures on May 17, 1995. Use the date as fixed and the loss as "roughly RMB 5–6 billion / multi-hundred-million USD" rather than picking a single number.

5. **8.10 incident — confirmed as Shenzhen, not Shanghai.** Important not to conflate: the 1992 riot over share-subscription forms was a Shenzhen Development Bank IPO, not a Shanghai event. Shanghai had its own lottery system but no comparable riot. Sources: [Wikipedia: Deng's Southern Tour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping's_southern_tour); [The China Project](https://thechinaproject.com/2022/12/21/something-old-something-new-the-1990-re-opening-of-shanghais-stock-exchange/).

6. **"上只角 / 下只角" sourcing relies partly on Chinese-language popular reference (Baidu Baike, Sohu, Sina blog).** These are reliable for the broad cultural meaning but should not be treated as primary scholarship. The Paper (澎湃) link is the most authoritative.

7. **A-Bao / 阿 prefix usage** is well-established as a southern Chinese (Wu / Cantonese / Min) diminutive but my sources do not unambiguously document that 阿 + given name is specifically *Shanghainese* (as opposed to broader Wu / southern Chinese). State as "Wu Chinese / southern Chinese" rather than "specifically Shanghainese."

8. **The list of stock-market terms** is verified across at least two sources for each term, but several terms (大户, 散户, 庄家, 涨停, 跌停, 牛市, 熊市) are general modern Chinese stock-market vocabulary, not exclusively 1990s. Their 1990s-Shanghai resonance is in the *intensity* of their use during the SSE's wild-west years, not exclusive origin. Make that distinction clear in the article.
