## What Dynasty Is Pursuit of Jade Set In? (Short Answer)

Pursuit of Jade (逐玉, 2026) is set in the **fictional Dayin Dynasty (大胤 / Dà Yìn)** — an entirely invented imperial state created for the source novel and carried over to the screen adaptation. It is *not* set in any real Chinese dynasty.

The Chinese character is **胤 (yìn — "descendant / heir")**, not the historical 殷 (Yīn, the alternate name for the Shang Dynasty c. 1600–1046 BCE). This is an important distinction: 大胤 is a fabricated word that *evokes* dynastic legitimacy without colliding with any real period. The drama draws visual and institutional inspiration primarily from the **Tang and Song dynasties**, with some Ming political-purge flavor layered on top. (Note: a small number of English-language fan write-ups have transcribed the dynasty as "Dayin" using the character 殷; the dominant Chinese-language sources, including the novel's own text and Sina/163 plot guides, consistently use 大胤.)

## The Fictional Dayin Dynasty (大胤朝)

### Name, source, and use across the franchise

The dynasty name **大胤 (Dà Yìn)** appears in:

- The original web novel *《侯夫人与杀猪刀》* (alternate title 《逐玉》, "The Marchioness and the Butcher's Knife") by **团子来袭 (Tuanzi Laixi)**, serialized on Jinjiang Literature City. The novel is hosted across Chinese reader sites (Kanunu, Quanben, Chamabooks) that consistently identify the setting as the 大胤 court.
- The 2026 Tencent / iQIYI / Netflix drama directed by Zeng Qingjie, which preserves the same fictional state.
- Plot analysis pieces on Sina News and 163.com that describe 大胤 as "a small court, internally unstable" (大胤是个小朝廷，朝局不稳).

The novel and drama share the *same* fictional setting — the drama did not invent the dynasty to dodge the novel's continuity. This is standard practice for Jinjiang costume-romance adaptations.

### Worldbuilding details

- **Capital + provinces:** "一京十二府" (one capital + twelve prefectures). The west occupies four prefectures.
- **Border state:** The northern enemy is **北厥 (Beique)**, a steppe / nomadic-coded antagonist whose name echoes the historical 突厥 (Turks/Türk Khaganate) — a frequent shorthand for "northern tribal threat" in Tang-flavored costume drama.
- **Inciting incident:** The 17-year-old "Jinzhou massacre" (锦州惨案), in which Crown Prince Chengde (承德太子) and the famed war-god Marquis of Wu'an, plus 8,000 soldiers, were lost on the battlefield after a botched supply run.
- **Power structure after the massacre:** Chancellor Wei Yan (魏严) installed a child emperor and consolidated power — a setup that lifts the *political shape* of late-Tang and Ming-era regency intrigue.

## Why a Fictional Dynasty? (Writers' Logic)

Costume dramas in mainland China have been gravitating toward **架空朝代 (jiakong chaodai — "skeletal / hollow dynasties")** since the mid-2010s, and Pursuit of Jade is squarely in this tradition. The reasons, drawn from industry analysis pieces and SAPPRFT (now NRTA) policy guidance:

1. **Regulatory cover.** After *Nirvana in Fire* (《琅琊榜》, 2015) became a hit using a fictional Liang state, screenwriters realized that **inventing a dynasty side-steps the National Radio and Television Administration's hostility toward "historical nihilism" (历史虚无主义)** — the official term for dramas that "distort, parody, or misrepresent" real historical figures or events. Fan-Knopf style guidelines explicitly require that costume fiction must be "complete and contain no obvious stylistic elements linking the story to a particular dynasty." A fictional dynasty satisfies this. ([NRTA / 文化和旅游部 — 扶持现实剧 严控古装剧](https://www.mct.gov.cn/whzx/whyw/201111/t20111130_706831.htm); [21财经 — 风光39年的古装剧退潮后](https://m.21jingji.com/article/20190703/herald/2b7965df5b691cd2be77ad7632e2abd6.html); [China Digital Times — "你懂的"国产剧审查](https://chinadigitaltimes.net/chinese/337088.html).)

2. **Plot freedom.** Writers can stage massacres, regicides, eunuch coups, and women-led armies without scholars protesting the chronology. The author Tuanzi Laixi has stated she was inspired by **late-Ming general Qin Liangyu (秦良玉)** — but rather than write a Ming-era period drama (which would force conformity to documented dates, ranks, and outcomes), she abstracted the character into a fictional setting where the heroine can rise from butcher's daughter to General Huaihua without contradiction. ([Tonboriday — author interview](https://www.tonboriday.com/2026/03/pursuit-of-jade-author-tuanzi-laixi.html); [Layar Hijau — Are characters based on real figures?](https://en.layarhijau.com/2026/03/10/are-tian-xiwei-and-zhang-linghes-characters-in-pursuit-of-jade-based-on-real-historical-figures/).)

3. **Aesthetic mixing.** Director Zeng Qingjie said outright that the production "does not pursue strict archaeological reconstruction but rather a xieyi-style (写意) Eastern aesthetic expression." A fictional dynasty lets the costume team blend Tang silhouettes, Song headwear, and Ming court politics without committing to one era. ([MyDramaList — Interesting facts](https://news.mydramalist.com/article/pursuit-of-jade-interesting-facts-every-fan-must-know).)

4. **Genre tradition.** Most prestige romance-costume dramas of the past decade have used fictional dynasties: *Nirvana in Fire* (Liang), *Joy of Life* (Southern Qing / 南庆), *Story of Yanxi Palace*'s spinoffs, *Love Between Fairy and Devil*, and most recent Jinjiang adaptations. Pursuit of Jade is following an established commercial playbook, not breaking from one.

## Real Chinese Dynasties That Inspired the Dayin Setting

Multiple Chinese-language and English-language reviewers converge on a layered reading: **the Dayin Dynasty is Tang-and-Song dominant in look, Ming in court politics**, with bits of Han and Warring States in the aristocratic titles.

### Tang influences (dominant for military / officialdom)

- **General Huaihua (怀化将军)**, the third-rank military honorific Fan Changyu receives in the finale, is a **real Tang Dynasty title (正三品下 — Regular Third Rank, Lower Grade)** within the 九品十六级 Tang military hierarchy. It sat just below the Huaihua Great General (怀化大将军, Regular Third Rank Upper). Historically the Tang awarded the title to surrendered tribal leaders to bind them to imperial rank. The drama uses it as a substantive command title rather than its strict historical honorific function. ([Sina — 唐朝军队九品十六级](https://k.sina.cn/article_7114080390_1a808408600100i859.html); [Sohu — 唐朝的武将等级](https://www.sohu.com/a/469152564_120904327).)
- The cosmopolitan, semi-foreign northern enemy 北厥 echoes the Tang's relationship with the 突厥 Turkic Khaganates.
- The "Joust" (簪花) ceremony — soldiers and officials having flowers pinned in their hats by the emperor at victory banquets — bridges late-Tang and Northern Song court ritual. The "Zanhua General" (簪花将军) nickname for Fan Changyu is drawn directly from this tradition. ([Newhanfu — Pursuit of Jade's Armor and Flowers](https://www.newhanfu.com/80923.html).)

### Song influences (dominant for visual identity)

- **Zhanjiao Futou (展脚幞头)** — the long-winged court headpiece worn by ministers in the drama — is *the* iconic Song Dynasty headwear, designed (per legend) to keep officials from whispering to each other during audiences.
- **Color hierarchy:** Officials of the third rank and above wearing purple at court is a Tang-Song convention preserved in the drama.
- The **Northern Song war-flower tradition** (emperor awarding "simhua" to victorious generals) directly informs the finale.
- The **hanfu silhouettes** — beizi outer robes, layered ruqun, modest crossover collars — read closer to Song than Tang or Ming.

Sources: [Newhanfu — Yan Yikuan in Pursuit of Jade](https://www.newhanfu.com/79960.html); [Newhanfu — Armor and Flowers](https://www.newhanfu.com/80923.html); [Hanfu Story — Pursuit of Jade Drama and Hanfu](https://thehanfustory.com/blogs/news/pursuit-of-jade-hanfu-fashion).

### Ming influences (dominant for political plot machinery)

- The **military purge / scapegoated general** plot recalls the Ming's well-documented purges of Yu Qian (于谦, 1457) and Yuan Chonghuan (袁崇焉, 1630).
- The author's stated model **Qin Liangyu (秦良玉, 1574–1648)** is a late-Ming Sichuanese general — the only woman in dynastic-era history to be officially appointed a regional commander and recorded in the *Ming Shi*'s "Generals" volume rather than the "Women" volume.
- The **Imperial Censorate (御史台)** as plot driver appears closer to Ming than Tang structure.
- Eunuch-faction politics, while present across many dynasties, has a Ming texture in the drama's portrayal.

### Other layers

- The **Marquis of Wu'an (武安侯)** title — Xie Zheng's hereditary rank — has a Han-to-Ming pedigree, *not* a Tang one. The title 武安侯 has been awarded as far back as the Warring States and was famously held by Liu Bang's Han-founding allies, Western Han external-relative Tian Fen (田蚡), Cao Wei prince Cao Shuang (曹爽), and Ming general Zheng Heng (郑亨). It carries a strong "cursed honor" reputation in Chinese folk history because many of its bearers died poorly — a thematic resonance that fits Xie Zheng's tragic backstory. ([Baidu Baike — 武安侯](https://baike.baidu.com/item/%E6%AD%A6%E5%AE%89%E4%BE%AF/5525252); [国家人文历史 — "武安侯"们的命运](https://www.gjrwls.com/people/20260326/1221828339879116800.html); [Sina Finance — 受封"武安侯",究竟是荣耀还是诅咒](https://finance.sina.com.cn/jjxw/2026-04-04/doc-inhtiymv7782322.shtml).)
- The "Marquis (侯)" rank itself is one of the **Five Ranks of Nobility (公侯伯子男)** that existed from Zhou through Qing — the drama uses it as a generic prestige rank without locking to one dynasty's exact administrative meaning.

### Verdict: dominant flavor

Pursuit of Jade reads **Tang-Song first, with a Ming political skeleton.** Hengdian World Studios — the principal filming location — is best known for its Tang and Song architectural reconstructions, and most journalist tours of the *Pursuit of Jade* sets describe palace interiors, courtyards, and imperial streets in those styles. ([Tatler Asia — 8 picturesque Pursuit of Jade locations](https://www.tatlerasia.com/lifestyle/travel/pursuit-of-jade-locations); [Tonboriday — All 8 filming locations](https://www.tonboriday.com/2026/02/all-8-pursuit-of-jade-filming-locations.html).)

## The 怀化将军 Title — Real Tang Military Rank

When Fan Changyu earns **General Huaihua (怀化将军)** in the drama's final arc, she is being given a name lifted directly from the **Tang Dynasty武散官 (military honorary officer) ladder**. Specifically:

- **Rank:** 正三品下 (Regular Third Rank, Lower Grade), one of the 16 graded military honorifics codified in the early Tang.
- **Sister title:** 怀化大将军 (Huaihua Great General, Regular Third Rank Upper Grade), which the drama also references in some translations of Fan Changyu's later promotion to "Huaihua Great General."
- **Historical function:** The Tang court used the 怀化 series specifically to bind newly submitted **non-Han tribal chieftains** (羁縻 — "loose-rein" frontier policy) to imperial rank structure. So the title's flavor is one of *frontier military prestige*, not a top-tier central court command — a clever fit for Fan Changyu, who is a butcher's daughter from the western borderlands rather than a central-capital aristocrat.

The drama strips the title of its historical "submitted tribal chief" connotation and uses it as a high-honor command rank for an ethnically Han female general, which is anachronistic but dramatically resonant — viewers familiar with Tang officialdom would recognize the rank as "real, third-grade, frontier-flavored."

Source: [Sina — 唐朝军队九品十六级](https://k.sina.cn/article_7114080390_1a808408600100i859.html).

## The 武安侯 Title and Aristocratic Hierarchy

Xie Zheng's hereditary rank is **Marquis of Wu'an (武安侯)** — a real, historically recurring Chinese aristocratic title. Key points:

- **Five-rank system:** 公 (Duke) > 侯 (Marquis) > 伯 (Earl) > 子 (Viscount) > 男 (Baron) is the Zhou-Han noble ladder that all later dynasties drew from. 侯 is the second tier.
- **The specific name "Wu'an" (武安):** literally "Military Pacification." Strongly associated with battlefield merit. The Wu'an title was first awarded in the Warring States era (Su Qin, Bai Qi, Li Mu all held the related 武安君 / "Lord of Wu'an"). Liu Bang held 武安侯 before founding the Han.
- **Recurring across dynasties:** Western Han (田蚡), Eastern Han (劉㥅), Cao Wei (曹爽), Ming (郑亨's eight-generation lineage).
- **"Cursed" reputation:** Multiple Chinese long-form essays on the title note that an unusually high proportion of 武安侯 bearers died violently or in disgrace — making it a perfect literary choice for Xie Zheng, whose father was framed and slaughtered, who himself spends 17 years hiding under an assumed name, and who walks a knife's edge until the finale.

The drama uses 武安侯 as a *generic high-prestige military marquisate* rather than implying any specific historical bearer. The title's vague-but-storied resonance is the point: viewers feel the weight without being tied to a particular century.

Sources: [Baidu Baike — 武安侯](https://baike.baidu.com/item/%E6%AD%A6%E5%AE%89%E4%BE%AF/5525252); [国家人文历史 — "武安侯"们的命运如何？](https://www.gjrwls.com/people/20260326/1221828339879116800.html); [Yzwb — "武安"魔咒](https://www.yzwb.net/news/wy/202603/t20260326_335707.html).

## Other Visual / Cultural Clues to the Era

- **Pheasant-feather helmet (雉尾盔)** worn by Xie Zheng: Production historian Huang Qiang (黄强) traced the headpiece to Ming-era zaju (杂剧 / theatrical) costuming, where pheasant feathers signaled bravery and were associated with stage characters like Lü Bu and Mu Guiying. Director Zeng called it a deliberate fusion of "stylized theatricality and televisual realism." This is **not** strict period dress — it is operatic-historical, signaling generic "valor-warrior" rather than a specific dynasty. ([Newhanfu — Zhang Linghe's Feathered Helmet](https://www.newhanfu.com/75992.html).)
- **Language register:** The drama's court Mandarin uses generic literary 文白 mixed register typical of fictional-dynasty dramas — no Tang poetics, no Ming colloquialisms.
- **Marriage rite:** Matrilocal marriage (入赘) was practiced across multiple dynasties (Han through Qing) but is most commonly associated in popular memory with Song and Ming peasant communities. The drama's use is generic.
- **Currency, weights, courtroom procedure:** Generic "imperial" — no era-specific markers.
- **Filming locations:** Hengdian World Studios (the Tang-Song palace, Ming Qing Imperial Palace replicas), plus Jinhua's Mu Garden, the Lingyin Temple area, and Wuxi's Three Kingdoms City. The architecture leans Tang-Song. ([Tonboriday — filming locations](https://www.tonboriday.com/2026/02/all-8-pursuit-of-jade-filming-locations.html).)

The pattern: fictional dynasties are not a quirk — they are the default setting for prestige romance-costume drama in 2020s mainland China. Hits that *do* commit to a real dynasty (e.g., *Serenade of Peaceful Joy* / 《清平乐》, Northern Song) face intense scrutiny from historians, hanfu communities, and the censorship board over accuracy. Fictional dynasties skip the controversy.

Pursuit of Jade fits squarely in the **historically-textured-but-fictional** sub-genre: more grounded than xianxia, less constrained than a true period drama.

## Verification Flags

1. **大胤 vs. 大殷 — important transliteration call.** Brief inputs assumed the dynasty character was **殷 (Yīn)**, evoking the Shang. Chinese-language primary and secondary sources (Sina plot guides, 163.com, Baidu Baike's novel summaries, source novel text) consistently use **胤 (yìn)**. Both characters are pronounced "yìn" in modern Mandarin, but they are different characters with different meanings — 殷 is the historical Shang dynasty name; 胤 means "descendant/heir" and is the standard fictional-dynasty placeholder character. **Recommendation: use 大胤 throughout the published article.** A small number of English-language fan write-ups have used 大殷; if cited, flag the discrepancy.

2. **"Inspired by Qin Liangyu" is well documented**, but the dynasty *itself* is not Ming. The author drew on Qin Liangyu as a *character archetype*, not as a Ming-period setting. Some downstream English sources confuse this.
