# Blossoms Shanghai (繁花) — Shanghainese vs Mandarin Research Brief

Research brief for the chineseidioms.com linguistic-moat article on Wong Kar-wai's *Blossoms Shanghai* (2023). Two complete dialect versions were produced: a Shanghainese (上海话, 沪语) version and a Mandarin (普通话) version. This brief documents the production decision, linguistic differences, ten-plus featured phrases, the opening narration, where to watch each version, the status of Shanghainese as a language, and critical reception of the dialect choice.

Prepared May 2026. All claims requiring caution are listed in the Verification Flags section at the end.

---

## The Two-Version Production Decision

*Blossoms Shanghai* is a 30-episode drama directed by Wong Kar-wai (王家卫), his first television project, adapted from Jin Yucheng's (金宇澄) 2012 novel *Blossoms* (繁花). The novel won the Mao Dun Literary Prize in 2015 and was written in a distinctive Shanghainese-inflected prose style. The drama premiered on December 27, 2023, simultaneously on CCTV-8 and Tencent Video. From day one, Tencent Video offered both a Shanghainese (沪语版) and a Mandarin (普通话版) audio track for every episode.

The Shanghainese version is widely considered the canonical or "auteur" cut. According to multiple Chinese-language production accounts, the entire series was shot live in Shanghainese — actors speaking the dialect on set — and the Mandarin track was created afterward in post-production by the same actors dubbing themselves. As one production report put it: "沪语是现场原音，普通话是后期配音" ("Shanghainese is the live on-set audio; Mandarin is the post-production dub"). Wong explicitly required that no third-party dubbing be performed — only the original Shanghainese and the official cast-dubbed Mandarin versions were authorized. This was framed as protection against "secondary creation" (二次创作).

Wong's stated artistic rationale is rooted in his personal biography. He was born in Shanghai in 1958 and emigrated to Hong Kong with his family at age five. Shanghai is, in his framing, the city of his lost early childhood; *Blossoms Shanghai* has been described — including in Wong's own MUBI program notes — as "a valentine to the city." For Wong, the dialect is not a stylistic flourish but, in the language his collaborators used, "the ecosystem" of the world Jin Yucheng built in the novel.

Crucially, the casting requirement followed from this. As early as March 2019, Wong publicly stated that "the cast crew must speak Shanghainese" (演职人员必须讲上海话). The principal cast — Hu Ge (胡歌, Ah Bao / 阿宝), Ma Yili (马伊琍, Ling Zi / 玲子), Tang Yan (唐嫣, Wang Xiaojie / 汪小姐), Xin Zhilei (辛芷蕾, Li Li / 李李) — are all Shanghai natives. Over twenty Shanghai Theatre Academy alumni were brought into supporting roles for dialect authenticity. Tang Yan has said publicly that *Blossoms Shanghai* was her first time performing an entire drama in Shanghainese.

**Did Tencent or CCTV require the Mandarin version?** This is the key institutional question, and the answer is yes — though not via a single explicit mandate. China's National Radio and Television Administration (国家广电总局) has issued multiple notices since 2000 directing broadcasters to use Putonghua as the basic broadcast language; a January 2014 order specifically directed announcers and hosts to use standard Mandarin and refrain from mimicking dialect pronunciation. In practice, satellite TV (卫视) channels cannot air a dialect-only drama in prime time without special permission. The dual-version structure is therefore both an artistic choice and a regulatory accommodation: the Mandarin version unlocked nationwide CCTV-8 broadcast, while Tencent Video — as an online platform with looser dialect rules — could carry the Shanghainese original. After the show's runaway online success, the Shanghainese version was exceptionally permitted on Dongfang Satellite TV (东方卫视) and aired in CCTV-8 afternoon reruns, which Chinese media described as an unusual concession.

## Shanghainese vs Mandarin: Linguistic Differences (Overview)

Shanghainese (上海话, also 沪语 / *Wu Chinese* family, ISO code: wuu) is not a "dialect" of Mandarin in the linguist's sense — it is a separate Sinitic variety with limited mutual intelligibility. Wu Chinese as a whole has roughly 70–80 million speakers (Wikipedia: Wu Chinese gives ~80 million; Sixth Tone uses ~70 million); Shanghainese itself accounts for around 15 million. The varieties retain a voiced–voiceless–aspirated three-way obstruent contrast that Mandarin merged away centuries ago, and they preserve Middle Chinese final stops as glottal stops, which Mandarin lost entirely.

For the *Blossoms Shanghai* audience, the most consequential differences are:

- **Pronouns.** Mandarin 我 (wǒ, "I"), 你 (nǐ, "you"), 他/她 (tā, "he/she"), 我们 (wǒmen, "we"), 你们 (nǐmen, "you all"), 他们 (tāmen, "they") map onto a completely different Shanghainese set: 我 *ngu*, 侬 *non*, 伊 *yi*, 阿拉 *aq-la* (or older 我伲 *ngu-gni*), 㑚 *na*, 伊拉 *yi-la*. So "you" in Shanghainese is written 侬, a character that in Mandarin reads *nóng* and means "I" in classical poetry — a fact that confuses many non-Wu viewers when they see Shanghainese subtitles.
- **The negator.** Mandarin uses 不 (bù). Shanghainese uses 勿 (*veq*) — a usage so distinctive that, per Wikipedia, it is sufficient on its own to classify a Sinitic variety as Wu.
- **Question particles.** Mandarin yes/no questions use 吗 (ma) or 吧 (ba). Shanghainese uses 伐 (*va*), as in "侬晓得伐？" ("Do you know?" — Mandarin: 你知道吗？).
- **Sentence rhythm.** Per the Wenxuecity production coverage, Jin Yucheng wrote the *Blossoms* novel in short Shanghainese-cadenced sentences; "Mandarin sentences run long; Shanghainese is the opposite" (上海话跟普通话的语气不一样，普通话的句子很长，但上海话相反). Reading the prose in Mandarin breaks the rhythm Jin built into it.
- **Vocabulary that has no clean Mandarin equivalent.** Words like 不响 *veq-shiang* ("non-response," used ~1,000 times in the novel) and 嗲 *dia* ("charming / cute / excellent," from English "dear") survive only in awkward Mandarin paraphrase. Linguist Qian Nairong (钱乃荣, Shanghai University) noted that even where a word survives the dub, it can carry different valence: 腔调 (qiāngdiào, "tone") in old Shanghainese carried a negative connotation; only after the 1980s did young Shanghainese repurpose it as praise ("personality," "style"). In *Blossoms Shanghai*, when the character Ah Bao tells someone "伊腔调勿要忒好噢" ("his style is something else"), the meaning only works in the young-Shanghainese register.

The cleanest summary comes from Chinese-language critical coverage: the Mandarin dub is "more peaceful," the Shanghainese original is "more assertive, smoother, more emotionally precise" (沪语的感觉更"强势"一些、更丝滑、融入地方语言特色，那个情绪更到位；普通话的感觉则更"平和"一些).

## 10+ Shanghainese Phrases from Blossoms Shanghai

For each phrase: characters, Shanghainese romanization (Wugniu where available; tone marks approximate), Mandarin equivalent, English gloss, and cultural context. Sources are noted inline; cross-verification status is in the Verification Flags section.

### 1. 不响 (veq-shiang)
- **Mandarin:** 不说话 / 沉默 (bù shuōhuà / chénmò)
- **English:** "not making a sound" — but loaded: a deliberate, knowing silence
- **Context:** The signature word of Jin Yucheng's novel — appearing roughly 1,000 times in the book per Sixth Tone, China Daily, and multiple Chinese sources. In the drama, Ah Bao says: "做生意要先学会两个字，不响。不该讲的、讲不清楚的、没想好的、没把握的、为难自己和为难别人的事情都不响" ("In business you first have to learn two words: *bu xiang*. Anything you shouldn't say, can't explain clearly, haven't thought through, aren't sure of, or that puts yourself or others in a bad spot — *bu xiang*"). Jin himself calls it "the philosophy of Shanghainese life — you know in your heart, but you don't speak; you watch the fire from across the river."

### 2. 嗲 (dia)
- **Mandarin:** 可爱 / 撒娇 / 优秀 (kě'ài / sājiāo / yōuxiù)
- **English:** "charming," "delightful," "excellent" — used for women, things, situations
- **Context:** Sixth Tone explicitly cites *dia* as a *Blossoms*-popularized term: "describes both beautiful women and something great." Per Chinese sources, the word is a phonetic borrowing of English "dear" from Shanghai's treaty-port era. There is no clean Mandarin equivalent; the Mandarin dub typically renders it as 嗲 with a softened delivery or substitutes 灵 *(ling)* / 好 *(hǎo)*.

### 3. 拉三 / 喇叭腔 (la-saq / la-baq-qiang)
- **Mandarin:** 放空话 / 食言 (fàng kōnghuà / shíyán)
- **English:** "talking big and not delivering" / "an unfulfilled promise"
- **Context:** Sixth Tone glosses *la-ba-qiang* (喇叭腔, literally "trumpet tone") as "implying an unfulfilled promise." Used in business-negotiation scenes when characters call out empty rhetoric. ⚠️ The exact form *la-ba-qiang* vs the more common Shanghainese 拉三 needs careful disambiguation — see Verification Flags.

### 4. 派头 (pa-deu) — first of the "Three Heads"
- **Mandarin:** 排场 / 气派 (páichǎng / qìpài)
- **English:** "presence," "swagger," "the way you show up"
- **Context:** Episode 1. Ye Shu (爷叔, played by 90-year-old You Benchang / 游本昌) instructs Ah Bao that doing business in Shanghai requires "三头": *pa-deu*, *xueq-deu*, *miao-deu*. The illustrative example: Ye Shu has Ah Bao rent a permanent English suite at the Peace Hotel on the Bund — the prestige of the address is itself a business asset.

### 5. 噱头 (xueq-deu) — second of the "Three Heads"
- **Mandarin:** 花招 / 卖点 (huāzhāo / màidiǎn)
- **English:** "gimmick," "hook," "the angle"
- **Context:** Same Ye Shu monologue. Cited example: arranging for Taiwanese singer Fei Xiang (费翔) to perform "Winter Fire" (冬天里的一把火) at a Shanghai department store as a marketing stunt. *Xueq-deu* has a secondary meaning in Shanghainese — sharp grooming, "neat hair" (头丝清爽) — that the Mandarin dub flattens out.

### 6. 苗头 (miao-deu) — third of the "Three Heads"
- **Mandarin:** 苗头 / 成果 (miáotou / chéngguǒ)
- **English:** "the upshot," "the actual result"
- **Context:** The point of *pa-deu* and *xueq-deu*, per Ye Shu, is to produce *miao-deu*. The full phrase 派头、噱头、苗头 has become one of the most quoted lines from the show in Shanghai business culture.

### 7. 一天世界 (yiq-thi-sy)
- **Mandarin:** 乱七八糟 (luànqībāzāo)
- **English:** "a complete mess"
- **Context:** Used by older characters (especially Ye Shu) to describe chaotic situations. Linguist Qian Nairong identifies it as part of the 1930s–1960s commercial-Shanghai vocabulary stratum Ye Shu carries. Lost entirely in the Mandarin dub, which renders it as standard 乱七八糟.

### 8. 死蟹一只 (sy-ha yiq-tsaq)
- **Mandarin:** 没救了 / 没办法 (méijiùle / méibànfǎ)
- **English:** "a dead crab" — i.e., done for, no way out
- **Context:** Per Qian Nairong's *Beijing News* analysis, the phrase carries four overlapping meanings in old Shanghainese: physically exhausted, deal gone bad, no escape route, hopeless. Used in business-collapse scenes by Ye Shu's generation.

### 9. 腔调 (qiang-diao)
- **Mandarin (literal):** 腔调 (qiāngdiào — "tone/manner")
- **English:** "style," "swagger," "the way someone carries themselves" — positive
- **Context:** Qian Nairong specifically cites a line — "伊腔调勿要忒好噢！" ("his style is something else!") — as a generational marker. In Ye Shu's older Shanghainese, *qiang-diao* would be sneering; in Ah Bao's 1990s usage, it is praise. The Mandarin dub can preserve the characters but cannot preserve the generational register.

### 10. 拎得清 (lin-deq-tshing)
- **Mandarin:** 明白事理 (míngbái shìlǐ)
- **English:** "you get it" / "you know how the world works"
- **Context:** A core Shanghai social virtue — being shrewd about other people's intentions and your own position. Recurring praise word for Ah Bao. Its opposite, 拎勿清 (*lin-veq-tshing*), is the Shanghainese equivalent of "clueless." The Mandarin dub renders it as 拎得清 but the term is borrowed from Shanghainese; native Mandarin speakers north of the Yangtze often need it glossed.

### 11. 门槛精 (men-khe-tshing)
- **Mandarin:** 精明 / 算计 (jīngmíng / suànjì)
- **English:** "doorway-shrewd" — calculating, knows every angle
- **Context:** Appears in Jin Yucheng's novel and recurs throughout the drama in commercial scenes. Often used about Wang Xiaojie's (汪小姐) bureaucratic maneuvering. In Mandarin, this is barely intelligible without translation; the Mandarin dub typically substitutes 精明.

### 12. 扎劲 (tsaq-jin)
- **Mandarin:** 厉害 / 带劲 (lìhai / dàijìn)
- **English:** "wow," "that's something," "killer"
- **Context:** Crowd-reaction word, used by side characters when Ah Bao pulls off a stock-market or business coup. Originally meant "well-dressed, showing off"; broadened to general approval. Highlighted in viewer reactions: many Sina/Sohu commenters wrote "沪语版太扎劲" ("the Shanghainese version is *too* tsaq-jin") about the show itself.

### 13. 侬 / 阿拉 / 伊 / 伊拉 — pronoun set
- **Shanghainese:** 侬 *non* (you sg.), 阿拉 *aq-la* (we), 伊 *yi* (he/she), 伊拉 *yi-la* (they)
- **Mandarin:** 你 / 我们 / 他·她 / 他们
- **Context:** Saturates every scene. Wikipedia notes that 阿拉 *aq-la* is itself a relatively recent import from Ningbo dialect that displaced an older Shanghainese 我伲 *ngu-gni* — so even within the show's diegetic 1990s present, you can hear generational pronoun shift. Ye Shu sometimes uses older variants.

### 14. 侬好 / 侬晓得伐？
- **Shanghainese:** *non-hau* / *non shiao-deq va?*
- **Mandarin:** 你好 / 你知道吗？
- **English:** "Hello" / "Do you know?"
- **Context:** The Shanghainese question-tag 伐 *va* (instead of Mandarin 吗 *ma*) is one of the most frequent dialect markers in casual dialogue and is preserved in the Mandarin dub's subtitles ("侬晓得伐？") for several characters as a cultural texture.

## The Opening Narration

The drama opens with a meta-textual prologue (引子). In a hotel room, Hu Ge as Ah Bao visits novelist Jin Yucheng — played by Jin himself, in cameo. Jin asks Ah Bao whether he has thought of a title for the book yet. Ah Bao says no — but he has the first sentence:

> **独上阁楼，最好是夜里。**
> *Duq-zaon koq-leu, tse-hau zy ya-li.* (Shanghainese, approximate)
> *Dú shàng gélóu, zuìhǎo shì yèlǐ.* (Mandarin reading)
> **"Climbing alone to the attic — best done at night."**

This is the actual opening line of Jin Yucheng's novel *Blossoms* (繁花). It is also the title under which Jin originally posted the work as serialized chapters on the Shanghai-based forum *Nongtang Wang* (弄堂网) starting May 10, 2011, under the pen name "独上阁楼" ("Alone in the Attic"). Jin has said publicly — including at the 2024 Hong Kong Book Fair — that this opening sentence was a deliberate echo of the final scene of Wong Kar-wai's 1990 film *Days of Being Wild* (《阿飞正传》), in which Tony Leung's character grooms himself in a Hong Kong attic. The narrative loop is therefore: Wong's film ends in an attic → Jin's novel begins with one → Wong's adaptation opens with Jin himself naming the line on screen.

**Important caveat on the dialect of this specific line:** Per *The Paper* (澎湃) and the China Writers Association analysis, the *voice-over narration* in the Shanghainese version of the drama is actually delivered in Mandarin; only the in-scene dialogue is fully in Shanghainese (the Paper: "《繁花》沪语版的旁白语言仍是普通话，但剧中的表演语言都不脱吴语范围"). So this opening line, as spoken by Jin Yucheng in cameo, is not the place to demonstrate the Shanghainese-vs-Mandarin contrast — but it is the show's most quoted single line.

## The Status of Shanghainese as a Language

Shanghainese is the largest Wu Chinese variety, with roughly 15 million speakers within a Wu family of ~70–80 million. It is mutually unintelligible with Mandarin. Per Wikipedia and Sixth Tone, today only about half of Shanghai's resident population can converse in Shanghainese, and another quarter can understand it; a 2021 survey found that 15.22% of respondents under 18 reported never using Shanghainese at all.

The decline has institutional causes. The Putonghua (普通话) standardization campaign was launched nationally in the 1950s, but the decisive turn for Shanghainese was in 1992, when Shanghai schools formally banned dialect use on campus. From the early 1990s, students could be punished for speaking Shanghainese; Sixth Tone documents a case as recent as 2011 in which a Shanghai middle-school student was forced to write an 800-character apology essay for speaking the dialect during class. A 2014 national broadcast directive ordered TV stations to use Mandarin or face de-listing. The cohort effect is sharp: Shanghai residents born before 1992 are mostly fluent; the proportion of fluent speakers declines exponentially with each post-1992 cohort.

Wu Chinese in general is classified by linguists tracking endangerment as among the most vulnerable major Sinitic varieties — partly because it lacks the institutional support Cantonese has from Hong Kong media and the global Cantonese diaspora. Preservation efforts include a Shanghai-government dialect-broadcast initiative on selected bus and subway lines, kindergarten Shanghainese rhymes, and grassroots projects like Wu Wei's WeChat-based Shanghainese resource platform (10,000+ members). Linguist Ding Dimeng (Shanghai University) warned in *China Daily*'s January 2024 coverage: "the dialect will become endangered if the government and society do not pay due attention to its preservation."

*Blossoms Shanghai*'s contribution to this landscape was real and measurable. Within the first weeks of release, Xiaohongshu (小红书) saw thousands of user-posted videos of people teaching themselves Shanghainese from the show. Fudan linguist Chen Zhongmin pointed out to China Daily that the last major Shanghainese TV drama before *Blossoms* "was probably back in the 1990s" — a roughly 30-year gap. Several Shanghai-area political delegates raised the question of optional Shanghainese language instruction in middle schools at the 2024 provincial 两会 sessions, citing the show by name.

## Critical Reception of the Dialect Choice

Reception split sharply along regional lines.

**Shanghai-based critics and the Yangtze-Delta audience** received the Shanghainese version as definitive. *Guangming Daily*'s arts column ran a piece titled "沪语版《繁花》，够味儿！" ("The Shanghainese *Blossoms* — that's the flavor!"). Linguist Qian Nairong (钱乃荣, Shanghai University), the leading academic specialist on Shanghainese, gave the show a positive evaluation in *The Beijing News* and *The Paper*, specifically praising Wong's decision to differentiate three generational registers — older speakers (Ye Shu's 1930s-vintage commercial Shanghainese), middle (Ah Bao's 1980s–90s mix), and younger — within a single drama. His one critique was that some younger actors' Shanghainese was, in his phrase, "腔调不要太浓" — "the accent shouldn't be laid on too thick." Other Shanghai-area pieces argued the Shanghainese version preserved the "地气" (groundedness) of the 1990s alleyway atmosphere that the Mandarin dub flattens.

**Non-Shanghainese mainland viewers** were more divided. Some embraced the Shanghainese version with subtitles as cultural immersion (the Xiaohongshu phenomenon); others preferred the Mandarin dub for accessibility. Douban and Zhihu threads about which version to choose ran into the thousands of replies. The general consensus pattern, per the Tencent News compilation: "上海的可能看沪语版的更有感觉，其他地方的看普通话的更容易接受" — "Shanghai viewers feel it more in the Shanghainese version; viewers from elsewhere find Mandarin easier."

**Hong Kong viewers** were the cold spot. TVB's *Blossoms* broadcast (June 2024) drew ratings of 19 points the first week, dropping to 14.6 and then ~10 — the lowest 2023–24 mainland-import drama performance on the channel. The dialect was part of the friction: Hong Kong audiences expecting Wong Kar-wai's Cantonese-language sensibility were watching either a Mandarin dub or a Shanghainese track they could not follow.

**International English-language reception** has been mostly art-house framed. Sixth Tone titled its piece "Shanghai on Screen: A Hit TV Show Helps a City Find Its Voice." The South China Morning Post ran an opinion piece — "Wong Kar-wai's *Blossoms Shanghai* is a kiss of life to a declining dialect." The Criterion Collection's introduction frames the show as part of Wong's lifelong artistic preoccupation with Shanghai. The dnyuz / NYT review (titled "In the Mood for Commerce") engages the show as a Wong work; the MCLC academic review and the Taylor & Francis open-access paper "Why dialect and region matter: the aesthetic effects of the defamiliarization techniques of Blossoms and its readers' reception" treat the dialect choice as central to the work's literary effect.

**Notable critical objection on the Mandarin side:** several Chinese-language critical pieces argued that the Mandarin dub felt mechanically post-produced — "味道尽失" (the flavor is entirely gone) for Shanghai-region viewers, even when delivered by the original actors. The Wenxuecity comparison: the same line, in Shanghainese, lands with edge and rhythm; in Mandarin, it goes flat.

## Verification Flags

Items below were not fully cross-verified across 2+ independent sources during this research pass. Fact-check before publication.

1. **Romanizations.** Wugniu romanization values for individual words (e.g., *veq-shiang*, *tsaq-jin*, *lin-deq-tshing*) are best-effort based on Wikipedia's Wugniu pinyin description and the Wu Chinese Society conventions. Tone marks have been omitted; for an article facing serious linguistics readers, consult the *Wu Chinese* (wu-chinese.com) site or the Wiktionary Wu Chinese entries for each specific term before locking copy.

2. **拉三 / 喇叭腔 (la-saq / la-baq-qiang).** Sixth Tone's gloss of *la-ba-qiang* as "an unfulfilled promise" appears in their *Blossoms Shanghai* piece. The form 喇叭腔 (literally "trumpet tone") is the form Sixth Tone uses; Chinese-language sources also discuss 拉三. These may or may not be the same Shanghainese expression. Confirm with a native Shanghai-area informant or Qian Nairong's dictionary before publishing.

3. **门槛精, 拎得清, 扎劲** — confirmed as common Shanghainese vocabulary; confirmed as appearing in *Blossoms*-adjacent commentary; **not** independently confirmed as said by a specific named character in a specific episode of the drama. They are framed in the brief as "recurring" / "used throughout" — accurate but not scene-pinpointed.

4. **The opening narration's exact wording.** "独上阁楼，最好是夜里" is confirmed as the opening line of Jin Yucheng's novel and as the line Jin Yucheng's cameo speaks in the drama's prologue. The exact full prologue dialogue between Hu Ge and Jin Yucheng was reconstructed from Chinese sources; the brief quotes one paraphrased exchange ("书的名字想好了吗?"/"没想好，不过第一句是：独上阁楼，最好是夜里"). Verify against episode 1 of the actual drama before publication.

5. **The opening voice-over is in Mandarin even in the Shanghainese version.** The Paper (澎湃) explicitly states this; it appears in only one source in this research pass. If the article positions "独上阁楼" as the iconic *Shanghainese* line, this caveat is critical to get right.

6. **Tencent / regulatory framing.** The framing that "Tencent required the Mandarin version for nationwide broadcast" is more accurately: *the National Radio and Television Administration's standing Putonghua-first directives effectively required a Mandarin track for CCTV-8 prime-time broadcast, which Tencent Video also carried.* I have no source showing Tencent itself imposed the requirement; the requirement is upstream of Tencent. Phrase carefully.

7. **TVB regulatory refusal.** The claim that "TVB originally requested Shanghainese-only per Wong's recommendation but was refused by local regulators" comes from one Chinese-language summary; I have not seen TVB or HK Communications Authority documentation. Worth a second source before printing.

8. **Disney+ / Netflix / Viki unavailability.** Stated as "not available" based on absence across all sources reviewed (Variety, Deadline, Indiewire, World of Reel, Wikipedia distribution section). Worth a final confirmation closer to publication date in case rights have shifted in 2026.

9. **Episode-1 attribution for the "三头" Ye Shu monologue.** Multiple sources agree this is an early-series moment. "Episode 1" specifically is widely reported but not directly confirmed in the sources I reviewed. Safe phrasing: "in the show's opening episodes" rather than "episode 1."

10. **Speaker counts for Wu Chinese.** Sixth Tone uses ~70 million, Wikipedia ~80 million, Wu Chinese: A Linguistic Gem ~80 million. The brief reports ~70–80 million as a range, which is accurate. Don't lock to a single figure.

11. **"嗲 from English 'dear.'"** This etymology is widely repeated in Chinese-language sources and in Sixth Tone-style coverage. Linguistically it is plausible (Shanghai's treaty-port era produced many such borrowings — e.g., 沙发 from "sofa") but is also contested by some linguists who derive 嗲 from native Wu sources. Phrase as "is said to derive from" or "popular etymology holds."

12. **Hu Ge's first-line "我想过无数次和她的重逢..."** appeared in one source as the "first line in the trailer," which is not necessarily the first line of the drama. Excluded from the main brief; do not use without re-verification.
